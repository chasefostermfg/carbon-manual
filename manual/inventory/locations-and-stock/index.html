Locations & stock

Warehouses, shelves, on-hand quantities and the item ledger.

Carbon always knows two things about your physical stuff: where it is and how much you have. This page explains how Carbon models your warehouse — locations, then shelves and bins inside them — how it tracks the on-hand quantity of every item, and how it keeps a complete history of every movement in the item ledger. You'll also learn to correct a count that's wrong (an inventory adjustment) and to verify stock with a cycle count.

Where this fits. Stock is the thing every other part of Carbon pushes and pulls. Receiving a purchase order adds stock; a job consumes raw material and produces finished parts; a shipment removes stock when it goes out the door. This page is the foundation underneath all of that — it's how Carbon answers "do we have it, and where?"

The two questions inventory answers

Throughout this page we'll follow Acme's stock of A36 Steel Plate 1″ (part number A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240) — the raw material that becomes the Counterweight Hook on job J000097. Acme buys it by the pound, stores it on a rack, and pulls 40 lb into every hook it makes.

Two simple numbers — 17,507 lb and bin R02-B01-01 — but Carbon tracks them for every item, in every location, and remembers every transaction that ever changed them. Let's build that up one layer at a time.

Layer 1 — Locations

A location is a physical site where you hold stock: a warehouse, a plant, a building. Most small shops run a single location; larger ones run several. Acme has two:

Locations matter because on-hand quantity is always per location. The same item can have 17,507 lb at Headquarters and a different amount in Texas, and Carbon never mixes them up. When you create a sales order, purchase order or job, you pick the location it draws from or delivers to — every hero-thread document for the Counterweight Hook uses Headquarters (loc_CZUGEFr…).

Moving stock between locations is a transfer, not an adjustment — Carbon has dedicated stock transfer and warehouse transfer records so the quantity that leaves one site is the exact quantity that arrives at the other. Reach for those when relocating physical stock; reserve adjustments (below) for correcting a count, not moving it.

Layer 2 — Shelves & bins (storage units)

Inside a location, Carbon lets you pinpoint exactly where something sits using storage units — the general term for any shelf, rack, row or bin. They can nest: a warehouse contains rows, a row contains bins. Acme's Headquarters mixes both styles:

  • Flat rack bins named by position — for example R02-B01-01 means Rack 02, Row B, Level 01, Position 01. Acme has many of these spread across racks R01–R05.
  • Nested storage — a broad "A Warehouse" zone that contains "Row H", which in turn contains a specific bin "H-12".

A storage unit is just a label for a place — naming it well (a consistent Rack-Row-Level-Position scheme like Acme's) is what makes a part findable on a busy floor. Each item can have a default storage unit, so Carbon suggests the right bin automatically when you receive or pick it. The A36 Steel Plate's default is R02-B01-01 (KgTNPORwzkxDG2z_wGLcI).

Item vs. storage unit. An item is what the thing is (A36 Steel Plate 1″). A storage unit is where a quantity of it sits (bin R02-B01-01). One item can live in several bins; one bin can hold several items. The combination — item × location × storage unit — is what Carbon counts.

Layer 3 — On-hand quantities

Now the numbers. Open any stock item and Carbon shows its on-hand quantity broken down by location, plus a set of related quantities that tell you what's already promised or inbound. Here's Acme's stock-on-hand list at Headquarters — the most-stocked materials and parts in the demo:

Reading an item's quantities tab

On-hand is only one of several numbers Carbon tracks. Open the Counterweight Hook and the quantities tab tells a fuller story: 100 finished hooks are sitting in stock, but 4,500 are already spoken for by open sales orders to Radiant Aerospace — and Carbon has two more jobs lined up to build them (J000098 for 1,500, in progress, and J000099 for 3,000, ready to start). That gap between what you have and what you owe is exactly what Carbon's replenishment logic watches.

Here's what each quantity means, in plain terms:

QuantityWhat it means
On handPhysically in stock right now — what you'd find if you walked out and counted.
On sales-order demandAlready promised to customers on open sales orders. Not yet shipped, but committed.
On production orderBeing made on an open job — will become on hand when the job finishes.
On purchase orderOrdered from a supplier and inbound — not arrived yet, so not on hand.
On holdPhysically present but quarantined — e.g. failed inspection, awaiting disposition.

Available is the number that actually matters for promising work: roughly on hand minus what's committed, plus what's confidently inbound. With 100 hooks on hand but 4,500 committed, Acme is deep in the hole on finished stock — which is fine, because the hook is a Make-to-Order part: Carbon builds each batch on a job rather than stocking ahead. For a buy-to-stock material like A36 plate, a low available number is the trigger to reorder.

The item ledger — every movement, forever

Behind those live quantities sits the item ledger: an append-only log of every single transaction that has ever changed an item's stock. Receipts, job consumption, job output, shipments, adjustments — each writes one entry. The on-hand quantity isn't stored as a guess; it's the running sum of the ledger. That's why Carbon's numbers reconcile: you can always trace a balance back to the events that produced it.

The rows above illustrate the shape of a ledger — a positive receipt, the 20,000 lb that job J000097 consumed to make 500 hooks (40 lb × 500), then a count that confirmed the balance. Every entry names the document that caused it, so the ledger doubles as an audit trail: "who moved this, when, and why" is never a mystery.

Batch & lot tracking (a quick note)

Some items demand more than a quantity — they need traceability down to the specific batch you received. A36 Steel Plate is one: it has requiresBatchTracking turned on. That means when you receive it, Carbon records a batch number (often the supplier's heat or lot number), and when a job consumes it, Carbon remembers which batch went into which finished parts.

  • A batch (or lot) is a quantity received together that shares a property — a steel heat, a chemical mix date, a casting pour.
  • Serial tracking goes one step further: every individual unit gets its own unique serial number (think a numbered turbine blade), rather than a shared batch.

The payoff is genealogy: months later you can answer "which heat of steel went into Radiant Aerospace's order?" — or, if a supplier recalls a heat, "which of our finished parts are affected?" — in seconds. Full detail lives in Tracking quality.

Fixing the numbers: inventory adjustments

Sometimes the physical count and Carbon's number disagree — a drop, a miscount, scrap that was never recorded. An inventory adjustment is how you make Carbon match reality. It writes one ledger entry (so the change is traceable) and posts the value difference to the Inventory Adjustment account (5310) in the books, keeping accounting honest too.

Open the item at the right location. Find the item (for example A36 Steel Plate 1″) and make sure you're looking at the correct location — adjustments are always per location. Adjusting at Headquarters never touches Texas.

Start a new adjustment. Choose Adjust quantity (sometimes labelled Positive adjustment / Negative adjustment). Pick the storage unit the change happened in — say R02-B01-01.

Enter the corrected quantity, not the delta. Tell Carbon what's actually there. If the system says 17,507 lb but you physically counted 17,450 lb, enter the new on-hand and Carbon computes the −57 lb movement for you. If the item is batch-tracked, pick which batch you're adjusting.

Record a reason and post. Pick a reason (damaged, miscount, found stock) and a note so the entry explains itself later. Posting writes the ledger entry, updates the on-hand quantity, and books the value change.

Adjustments are for corrections, not routine flow. Don't adjust stock down to "use it up" — that's what consuming material on a job is for — and don't adjust it to move stock between sites — that's a transfer. Misusing adjustments hides the real reason stock changed and muddies your costs. Each adjustment should answer a clear "why was the count wrong?"

Verifying the numbers: cycle counts

A cycle count is a scheduled physical count of part of your inventory — a few bins at a time, rotating through everything over weeks, rather than shutting the whole shop for one big annual count. The goal is to catch drift early and keep the ledger trustworthy without stopping production.

Decide what to count. Pick a slice — one rack, one item class, or your highest-value materials. Acme might count the steel racks (R02-B01-*) one week and the stainless racks the next. Counting your costliest stock most often (an "ABC" approach) catches the errors that matter most.

Print or pull the count sheet. Carbon lists what it thinks is in each bin. A counter walks the floor and writes down what's actually there — ideally without seeing the expected number, so the count stays honest.

Enter the counted quantities. Type each counted figure back in. Carbon compares it to the expected on-hand and flags any variance. A bin with no variance needs nothing further.

Reconcile variances into adjustments. For each bin that differs, Carbon posts an inventory adjustment (the same mechanism as above) so the ledger, the on-hand quantity, and the books all move together. After posting, Carbon's number once again matches the shelf.

Treat repeat variances as a signal, not just a number to fix. If the same bin is always short, the cause is usually upstream — material consumed but never recorded on a job, scrap that skipped the system, or a picking step done by hand. Counting tells you that you're off; the item ledger tells you why.
