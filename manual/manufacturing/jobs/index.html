Jobs

Create production jobs from a sales order or from scratch.

A job is a production order: Carbon's instruction to the shop floor to make a specific quantity of a part by a specific date. If a sales order is the promise you made to a customer, the job is how you keep it. This page follows one real job — J000097, where Acme makes 500 Counterweight Hooks for Radiant Aerospace — so you can see exactly how a job is born, what it carries, and how you turn it loose on the shop floor.

Where this fits. A job sits in the middle of the end-to-end flow: Quote → Sales Order → Job → Purchasing → Quality → Shipment → Invoice. The job we follow here comes straight from sales order SO000069 and feeds purchase order PO000057 (the steel it needs). Once it's released, operators run it in the shop-floor app, and you track its day-to-day progress in managing production.

What a job is

When you make something rather than buy it, that work has to be planned, tracked, costed and finished. A job is the record that holds all of it together. A single job answers:

  • What are we making? (the part — here, CWH-A36-15, the Counterweight Hook)
  • How many? (the production quantity — 500 each)
  • By when? (the due date — 2026-06-26)
  • How is it made? (the method — a recipe of materials and steps)
  • Where are we? (the status, and how many good pieces are done)

The big idea: a job carries its own copy of the method. When the job is created, Carbon snapshots the part's bill of materials and routing onto the job. From that moment the job is self-contained — even if someone later edits the part's master method, this job keeps the recipe it was created with. That's what lets you trust a finished job's cost and traceability months later.

Method = BOM + Routing. The bill of materials (BOM) is what the part is made of; the routing is the ordered steps to make it. A job copies both. We cover authoring methods in depth in Methods: BOM & routing.

How a job gets created

There are two ways a job comes into being. Most jobs at Acme are created the first way, without anyone lifting a finger.

1. Automatically, from a Make-to-Order sales order line

When a sales order line is set to Make to Order — meaning "don't ship this from stock, make it fresh for this order" — Carbon offers to spin up a job for that line directly from the sales order. The job is pre-filled with the part, the quantity, the customer and the due date from the order, and it stays linked back to the line it came from.

This is exactly how J000097 was born. Look at the source line on SO000069:

Because the line says Make to Order, Carbon links job J000097 to it. The job knows it's working for SO000069, and the order knows the job is making its 500 hooks. You never typed the part number or quantity into the job — they flowed in from the order.

The same automatic link happens for the other two Radiant orders for this part: SO000070 created job J000098 (1,500 EA) and SO000071 created job J000099 (3,000 EA). One part, three orders, three independent jobs.

2. Manually, when you need a job on its own

Sometimes you want a job that isn't tied to a customer order — for example, building parts to stock, a rework batch, or a one-off prototype. In that case you create the job yourself. Acme's job J000094 (10,000 Thermal Exhaust Nozzles, built to stock) is a manual job like this; it has no sales order behind it.

Open the Jobs list and start a new job. Go to the Jobs area under the production module. You'll see every job in the system — Acme has 89 of them, J000001 through J000099. Click New Job.

Pick the part. Choose the item you want to make — for example CWH-A36-15, the Counterweight Hook. Carbon checks that the part is a Make item (its replenishmentSystem is Make, not Buy) and pulls in its description and unit of measure (EA).

Set the quantity, due date and priority. Enter how many to make, when they're needed, and how urgent the job is relative to others. We'll look at each of these fields next.

Save. Carbon assigns the next job number and snapshots the part's method onto the new job. The job opens in the Planned status, ready for you to review before you release it.

The fields that drive a job

When you open J000097 you land on its Details tab. A handful of fields do most of the work; everything else on the shop floor flows from them.

Production quantity is how many good pieces you intend to finish — here, 500. If you expect to lose some to scrap, you can also set a separate scrap quantity so Carbon plans enough material; J000097 ran with a scrap quantity of 0.

Due date is when the parts must be done. The deadline type tells Carbon how to treat that date: a Hard Deadline (used here) is a firm commitment, versus a soft target. This feeds the scheduling and the at-a-glance "is this job late?" view in managing production.

Priority ranks this job against every other open job competing for the same machines and people. J000097 is priority 1 — the highest — so it jumps the queue at the burn table. Lower-priority jobs wait their turn.

Location matters. A job is made at one location. J000097 runs at Headquarters, so it can only use Headquarters work centers (like Burn Table — PA2) and pulls material from Headquarters stock. Acme's other location, Texas, has its own burn tables and laser tables.

The method the job carries

Click the Method tab and you'll see the recipe Carbon copied onto the job. For the Counterweight Hook it's short: one material, three operations. The root node is the part itself; everything indented under it is what goes into making it.

Two things to notice. First, the BOM has a single material line: 40 LB of A36 steel plate per piece. Multiply by 500 and the job needs 20,000 LB of steel — Carbon does that math for you. Second, the material is set to Pull from Inventory, meaning the job expects to draw the steel from stock; the purchase order to Ryerson exists to make sure that stock is there.

Because the job carries its own copy of this method, you can tweak it for this run only — swap a material, add a deburring step, change a time — without touching the part's master method or any other job. Edits here affect J000097 and nothing else.

Operations: the steps, in order

The Operations tab lists the routing as a sequence. Each operation runs at one work center (a specific machine or station) and has a time per piece. Operations run in order — each one here is set to start After Previous, so Sandblasting can't begin until Flame Cut is done.

Every operation here is Inside (done in Acme's own shop) rather than outside (subcontracted). The labor time is per piece, so Carbon can estimate the total: 45 minutes of flame cutting × 500 pieces is the bulk of the work, with a quick sandblast and a final inspection gate at the end. As operators clock on and report finished pieces in the shop-floor app, the Complete and Status columns fill in — that's how all three reached Done and the whole job rolled up its real recorded cost.

The last operation, Inspection, is a quality step. Putting an inspection at the end of the routing makes it a gate: the parts aren't considered finished until they pass. See Tracking quality.

Materials: what the job will consume

The Materials tab is the job's shopping list — every material it needs, with how much it expects to use. For J000097 it's one line:

The steel is batch-tracked, so when it's consumed Carbon records exactly which heat of material went into this batch of hooks — the foundation of Radiant's traceability. The material is pulled from bin R02-B01-01 at Headquarters.

Releasing a job to the floor

A new job starts life as Planned — a draft you can still edit freely. Nothing reaches the shop floor until you release it. Releasing is the deliberate "go" that says this job is real, schedule it and let operators start clocking on.

Review the job. Confirm the quantity, due date and priority on Details, and skim the Method, Operations and Materials tabs to make sure the recipe is right for this run.

Check material availability. Carbon flags any shortages. For J000097, the 20,000 LB of A36 steel wasn't all on hand, which is why Acme raised purchase order PO000057 to Ryerson. Buying and making can run in parallel — you don't have to wait for steel to release the job.

Release the job. Click Release. Carbon stamps the released date — for J000097 that was 2026-06-11 — moves the job into the active queue, and makes its operations available in the shop-floor app.

The floor takes over. Operators clock onto each operation in sequence and report finished pieces. When the last operation's good quantity reaches the production quantity, the job is Completed — J000097 completed the same day, with all 500 pieces done and later shipped.

Job statuses

A job moves through a clear set of statuses as it goes from idea to finished parts. Acme's 89 jobs are spread across all of them right now:

Here's what each status means and how a job moves between them:

StatusWhat it means
PlannedCreated but not yet released. A draft — edit quantity, dates and the method freely. Not visible to the shop floor.
ReadyReviewed and queued to start — material and capacity are lined up, waiting for its turn (e.g. J000099).
In ProgressReleased and being worked. Operators are clocking time and reporting pieces (e.g. J000098, J000095).
CompletedAll good pieces finished and received to inventory. The job's true cost is final (e.g. J000097).

A Planned job does nothing on its own. It won't appear on the shop floor and operators can't book time to it until you release it. If a job seems "stuck" with no activity, the first thing to check is whether it's still in Planned.

Where the numbers go next

Once J000097 completed, its 500 finished hooks were received into inventory, shipped to Radiant Aerospace, and billed on invoice AR000025. The job's recorded labor and material rolled up into the cost of those parts — closing the loop between what you planned and what it actually took.
