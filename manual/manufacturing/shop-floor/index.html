The shop floor (MES)

How operators run jobs: clock in, record quantities and complete operations.

So far the manual has planned the work — quoting it, ordering it, releasing a job. This page is where the work actually gets done. It covers Carbon's MES — the Manufacturing Execution System — the part of Carbon a machine operator opens at a station on the shop floor. The MES turns a planned job into real activity: an operator picks an operation, clocks on, follows the written instructions, records how many good and scrap pieces they made, and clocks off. Every one of those taps feeds real labor time and cost back to the job — no clipboards, no end-of-day data entry.

Where this fits. A job is planned by a scheduler and run by operators. Managing production is the manager's view — releasing and tracking jobs. This page is the operator's view: the MES is the screen on the floor where the hours actually get logged. The last operation here is usually a quality gate, which leads into tracking quality.

The MES is its own app

Carbon ships as two front ends that share one database:

  • The ERP — the full office app you've seen on every other page (Sales, Purchasing, Items, Production). This is where jobs are planned, scheduled and costed.
  • The MES — a stripped-down, touch-friendly app built for a shop-floor tablet or kiosk. It shows an operator only what they need: the operations waiting at their work center, the instructions for each, and big buttons to start, pause and finish.

Both look at the same job. When an operator finishes an operation in the MES, a manager sees it update in the ERP a moment later. You don't enter anything twice.

Operators reach the MES at its own address (think mes.carbon.ms) and sign in with their own account. A worker who only runs machines never needs to learn the full ERP — they live entirely in this one focused screen. Permissions are covered in Users & permissions.

We'll follow the same real job as the rest of the manual: J000097, the batch of 500 Counterweight Hooks (CWH-A36-15) for Radiant Aerospace. It runs three operations in order — Flame Cut, Sandblasting, then Inspection — and Carbon recorded the whole thing finishing in about 282 seconds of logged shop time.

What an operator sees

When an operator signs in, the MES doesn't show them the whole factory. It shows the operations queued at the work center(s) they're assigned to, newest deadlines first. Each row is one operation on one job. Operator Fred Flame works the burn tables, so his queue is led by the Flame Cut on J000097.

A couple of things to notice:

  • The MES filters by work center, not by job. Fred sees Flame Cut work from several different jobs together, because they all run on his Burn Table. A different operator at the Sand Blasting Room (here, Carlos Axis) sees a completely different list.
  • An operation only becomes Ready once the operation before it is done (the routing on J000097 says each step runs After Previous). Sandblasting won't show as Ready in the blast-room queue until Flame Cut is finished. This is why the same job's three operations light up one at a time, in sequence.

Carbon defines an operation as one step in a job's routing, performed at one work center. J000097 has three: Flame Cut at Burn Table - PA2, Sandblasting at the Sand Blasting Room, and Inspection at the Inspection Station. Each has a planned time per piece, which is what Carbon expects the operation to take.

Run an operation, start to finish

Here's the full loop an operator does for one operation. Fred will run the Flame Cut on J000097: 500 hooks, 45 minutes planned per piece, on Burn Table - PA2.

Open the operation

Tap the Flame Cut row for J000097. The MES opens an operation card — everything for this one step on one screen. It tells the operator what to make, how many, from what, and how.

The Materials tab shows what to pull and from where — for this part, 40 LB of A36 Steel Plate 1″ per piece, drawn from bin R02-B01-01. The Tools tab lists any tooling or fixtures the step needs. Because the steel is batch-tracked, the operator will be asked which batch they consumed — that's what makes full traceability possible later.

Read the standard procedure

Most operations have a procedure attached — Carbon's name for a standard work instruction. Acme keeps 12 procedures, one for each kind of process, so the same job done by two different operators is done the same way. The procedure for a Flame Cut / CNC-style operation walks through setup and run as a checklist the operator ticks off:

Steps aren't only checkboxes. A procedure step can also ask the operator to type a measurement (with an allowed minimum and maximum, so an out-of-range value is caught on the spot), pick from a list, or attach a photo or file. That's how a written instruction becomes recorded, checkable data instead of a laminated sheet on the wall.

Procedures are versioned. Acme's Quality Inspection procedure, for example, has an Active version 1 and a newer Draft version 2 — operators always run the Active version, and managers can revise the draft without disrupting the floor. Build and manage them from the ERP's Production module.

Clock ON

When the operator is ready to cut, they tap Start. This is the moment that matters: it clocks them ON to the operation and Carbon starts a timer. From here, every minute counts against this job — at Burn Table - PA2's labor rate of $50/hour, that time is becoming real cost on J000097 as the operator works.

If the operator stops for a break or to switch tasks, they tap Pause — the timer stops, so breaks don't get billed to the job. They can clock off one operation and onto another at any time; Carbon keeps each operation's time separate.

An operator can only be clocked on to one operation at a time. If Fred forgets to clock off Flame Cut before lunch, his time keeps running against J000097. Encourage the habit: Pause when you step away, Finish when the step is done. A manager can correct a bad time entry later from the ERP, but it's far cleaner to get it right on the floor.

Record good and scrap quantities

As pieces come off the table, the operator reports them. Carbon tracks two numbers:

  • Good quantity — pieces that passed and move on to the next operation.
  • Scrap quantity — pieces that failed and won't continue. Each scrap is tagged with a reason so the shop can see why parts are being lost.

Acme keeps three scrap reasons — Damaged, Defective, and Quality — and the operator picks one whenever they scrap a piece.

On J000097 every hook was good — 500 made, 0 scrapped — so the job's scrapQuantity stayed at zero. When scrap does happen, those reasons roll up into reports so a manager can spot, say, a run of Defective parts at one work center and dig into the cause.

Scrapping a piece does more than count it. It can also feed the quality side of Carbon — repeated failures of the same kind are exactly what a non-conformance (NCR) is for. Scrap reasons and failure modes are the raw material for quality analysis.

Clock OFF and complete the operation

When the count is in and the work is finished, the operator taps Finish. This clocks them off, stops the timer, and marks the operation Done. The finished good quantity is now available to the next operation — so the moment Fred finishes Flame Cut, Sandblasting flips to Ready in the blast-room queue.

The other two operators do the same thing in turn. Carlos Axis runs the Sandblasting operation (2 min/piece) at the Sand Blasting Room and clocks off; then Terry Tolerance, the inspector at the Inspection Station, runs the Quality Inspection procedure (10 min/piece) and clocks off the last operation.

What clocking off feeds back

Each clock-on/clock-off pair is a production event — a timestamped record of who worked which operation, for how long, and how many good and scrap pieces resulted. These events are the heart of the MES. They do three jobs at once:

  1. They cost the job. Recorded minutes are multiplied by the work center's labor and machine rates (Burn Table - PA2 bills $50/hr labor + $25/hr machine) and posted to the job as actual cost. That's how Carbon compares what a job was estimated to cost against what it really cost — and the gap shows up as a variance you can act on.
  2. They advance the work. Reported good quantity flows to the next operation; the routing's After Previous rule means the floor naturally runs steps in order.
  3. They build the record. Who ran each step and when is stored for good — useful for traceability, payroll, and answering "what happened on this job?" months later.

Finishing the last operation finishes the job

The operations run in sequence, and the last one is special. When the inspector clocks off the final Inspection operation on J000097, there's nothing left to do — all three operations are Done and all 500 pieces are made. Carbon rolls the job up: the finished hooks are received into inventory, the job's actual cost is locked in from the production events, and the job moves to Completed.

This is the hand-off back to the office: a Completed job means the parts exist, ready to be picked and shipped against sales order SO000069 and billed on invoice AR000025. The shop floor did its part, and every minute it spent is now visible to the rest of Carbon.

Why clock time at all? Because guessed labor is the most common reason a shop loses money without knowing it. When operators clock real time on the MES, every quote you build next rests on what work actually takes — not a hopeful estimate. The MES is where Carbon's costing gets honest.
