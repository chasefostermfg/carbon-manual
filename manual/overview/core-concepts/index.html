Core concepts

The handful of ideas that make everything else click: items, methods, jobs, orders and traceability.

Carbon has a lot of screens, but it's built on a small set of ideas. Learn these eight and the rest of the manual is just detail. This page defines each one in plain language and shows it on the same real example you met in the walkthrough — a Counterweight Hook that Acme makes for Radiant Aerospace. Read it once, top to bottom, and the vocabulary you'll see everywhere else in Carbon will already make sense.

Before you start. This is the mental-model page. You don't need to do anything in the app yet — just read along. If you'd rather see the whole flow in motion first, the 15-minute walkthrough follows one order from quote to cash. Either order works; this page goes a level deeper on the why.

The big picture

Almost everything in Carbon is a record — an item, a quote, a job, a purchase order — and the records link to each other. You enter information once, and it follows the work everywhere it goes. Here's how the main records connect for our hook:

1. The Item

An item is anything you make, buy, store, or sell. It's the noun the whole system is built around. Every part on a quote, every material on a purchase order, every line on an invoice points back to an item.

Our hero is the item CWH-A36-15, the Counterweight Hook. Two settings on it matter most:

  • Item type — what kind of thing it is (see the six types below).
  • Replenishment — how you get it: you either make it or buy it (covered in section 3).

The six item types

Every item is exactly one of these types. The type tells Carbon how the item behaves — what screens it shows, whether it can have a recipe, and where it shows up.

TypeWhat it isAcme example
PartA finished or in-process item you make or buy and sell or use in another part.CWH-A36-15
MaterialRaw stock consumed to make parts — sheet, bar, billet, tube.A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240
ToolA reusable instrument used on the floor; not consumed by a job.TOL-CAL (Digital Calipers)
ConsumableA supply used up during work but not part of the finished item.GRD-WHL (Grinding Wheels)
ServiceOutside work or labor you buy or sell rather than a physical thing.Anodizing, plating, freight
FixtureA jig or holding device that positions a part during an operation.A weld fixture or burn-table jig

Don't overthink the type at first. The two you'll use most are Part (the things you sell) and Material (the raw stock you cut them from). Full detail on every field lives in Defining an item.

Unit of measure and tracking

Two more item settings show up constantly:

  • Unit of measure (UoM) — how you count it. The hook is counted in EA (Each); its steel is measured in LB (pounds). Carbon converts between a purchase UoM and a stock UoM when they differ.
  • Tracking type — whether Carbon keeps a stock balance for it. The hook is Inventory tracked (Carbon counts how many are on hand). Some items are Non-Inventory (bought straight to a job and never counted on a shelf).

2. The Method — BOM + Routing

A method is the recipe for a made item. It has two halves, and you'll hear both terms constantly:

Bill of Materials (BOM) — what the item is made of. The list of materials and sub-parts that go into one unit, with quantities. The hook's BOM is one line: 40 lb of A36 steel plate.

Routing — how the item is made. The ordered list of operations (shop steps), each run at a work center. The hook's routing is three operations: Flame Cut, then Sandblasting, then Inspection.

Two things to notice, because they explain a lot of Carbon's "magic":

  • The method drives cost and price. Carbon adds up the material (40 lb of steel) and the shop time (45 + 2 + 10 minutes per piece, at each work center's labor and machine rates) to estimate what one hook costs to make. That estimate is what you price a quote against.
  • The method drives the plan. When you make this part, Carbon copies the method onto the job — so the shop knows exactly what to consume and which steps to run, in order.

A method can nest. If a part is built from sub-assemblies that you also make, each one brings its own BOM and routing, and the tree grows another level deep. The hook is simple — one material, three steps — but the same structure scales to complex assemblies. See Methods: BOM & routing.

3. Make vs Buy (replenishment)

Replenishment answers one question for every item: when you need this, how do you get more? There are exactly two answers, and they decide which part of Carbon does the work.

ReplenishmentWhat it meansWhat the item needsDemand creates a…
MakeYou produce it in-house.A method (BOM + routing).Job
BuyYou purchase it from a supplier.A supplier and a price.Purchase Order

This is why our two items behave differently:

  • The hook CWH-A36-15 is Make — so demand for it produces a job (section 5).
  • Its steel A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240 is Buy — so demand for it produces a purchase order (section 7).

A single order can pull both levers at once: making the hook creates demand for the steel, which Carbon then suggests buying. You'll see that chain play out below.

Replenishment isn't only Make-vs-Buy — it also governs when and how much to reorder (reorder points, lead time, lot sizing). Those settings live in Replenishment & planning.

4. Demand — Quote and Sales Order

Everything Acme makes or buys starts with demand: someone wants something. On the sell side, demand comes in two records that link together.

Quote — a priced offer, before the customer commits. You add lines (item, quantity, price) and send it. Carbon's configurators can even price made-to-order parts from the customer's choices. Our hook was quoted as Q000061.

Sales Order (SO) — the customer's commitment. It records who's buying, what, how many, when it's due, and at what price. When a quote is accepted you convert it to a sales order in one click — no re-typing. Our quote became SO000069.

The fulfillment method on the line — Make to Order — is the hinge between selling and making. Because the hook is a Make item, that setting tells Carbon the next step is a job, not a pick from the shelf. Learn the sell side in Quoting and Sales orders.

5. The Job — a production order

A job is a production order: the instruction to actually make a quantity of a part. When you release a job, Carbon copies the item's method onto it, so the job carries its own snapshot of the BOM and routing. (That copy is why changing a part's master method later doesn't rewrite jobs already in progress.)

For our order, Carbon created job J000097 for 500 hooks. The operations are the routing from the method; the shop floor reports against them.

A job moves through statuses as it goes — Draft → Ready → In Progress → Completed (you'll also see Planned for jobs scheduled but not yet released). The same hook runs on other jobs too: J000098 (1,500 EA, In Progress) and J000099 (3,000 EA, Ready) are queued for Radiant right behind this one. Full detail in Jobs; the operator side is the shop floor (MES).

6. Supply — the Purchase Order

A job needs materials. The hook's BOM calls for 40 lb of A36 steel per piece — 20,000 lb for the batch of 500 — and the steel is a Buy item. So Carbon raises supply with a purchase order.

Purchase Order (PO) — your commitment to a supplier to buy specified items at agreed prices and dates. It's the buy-side mirror of a sales order. The steel for our hook was ordered on PO000057 from Ryerson.

When the steel arrives, a receipt adds it to inventory. That single act does two things: it raises the on-hand balance, and — because A36 plate is batch-tracked — it records exactly which batch of steel came in. Hold that thought; it's the foundation of traceability in section 8. Learn the buy side in Purchasing basics.

7. Location and Shelf — where stock lives

Physical items have to be somewhere. Carbon answers "where?" at two levels of detail:

Location — a site or warehouse. Acme has two: Headquarters and Texas. Stock, work centers, and orders all belong to a location. Everything in our hero thread happens at Headquarters.

Shelf (also called a bin or storage unit) — a specific spot inside a location: a rack, shelf, or bin. Acme's A36 steel lives on shelf R02-B01-01; finished hooks land on shelf 8-1-H.

Knowing the exact shelf is what lets the shop pick material fast and lets Carbon tell you not just how much you have but where it is. More in Locations & stock.

8. Traceability — the genealogy

The last big idea ties the others together. Traceability (or genealogy) is Carbon's ability to answer, after the fact: what exactly went into this? and where did this end up?

It works because of two kinds of tracking you can turn on for an item:

TrackingWhat it recordsWhen you use it
BatchA lot number shared by many units made or received together.Bulk material like steel plate, paint, chemicals.
SerialA unique number for each individual unit.High-value or regulated parts tracked one-by-one.

A36 steel plate is batch-tracked. So when Ryerson's steel was received against PO000057, Carbon stamped that incoming lot with a batch number. When job J000097 consumed the steel, Carbon linked that exact batch to those exact 500 hooks — and through the sales order and shipment, to Radiant Aerospace.

Loading diagram...

That unbroken chain is worth real money. If Ryerson later reports a defect in that heat of steel, Acme can answer "which finished parts are affected, and which customer got them?" in seconds — instead of recalling everything. The same chain runs the other way too: from a customer complaint back to the raw material. Quality records like inspections and non-conformances hang off this genealogy.

You don't have to track everything. Turn on batch or serial tracking only for items where it earns its keep — regulated, high-value, or warranty-sensitive parts. For everything else, plain inventory tracking is enough.
