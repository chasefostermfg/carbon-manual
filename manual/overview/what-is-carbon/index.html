What is Carbon?

ERP, MES and QMS in one system of record — and who uses each part.

Carbon is software for running a manufacturing business. If you've never used a tool like this before, here's the short version: Carbon keeps track of everything your shop does — the orders you take, the parts you make, the materials you buy, and the money that moves — all in one place. When you write something down once, Carbon carries it everywhere it's needed, so nobody has to re-type it.

Most shops cobble this together from spreadsheets, a separate accounting program, paper travelers on the shop floor, and a folder of inspection forms. Each one holds part of the truth, and none of them talk to each other. Carbon replaces that pile with a single system of record — one trusted place where the real, current state of the business lives.

Where this fits. This is the first concept page. It explains what Carbon is at a high level. Next, Core concepts defines the building blocks (items, methods, jobs, orders), and Navigating Carbon shows you how to move around the app. If you'd rather see it all in action first, the 15-minute walkthrough follows one real order end to end.

Three jobs, one system

People often describe business software with three acronyms — ERP, MES, and QMS. Plenty of companies buy three separate programs to cover them. Carbon does all three, and the magic is that they're not three programs bolted together: they're three views of the same data. Here's what each one means in plain language.

ERP — run the business

Sales, purchasing, inventory and costing. The office side: who ordered what, what you need to buy, what's in stock, and what it all costs.

MES — run the shop floor

Releasing jobs, clocking onto operations, and recording finished pieces. The plant side: turning orders into real, made parts.

QMS — keep it good

Inspections, non-conformances (NCRs) and full traceability. The quality side: proving each part is right and tracing where it came from.

ERP — the business

ERP stands for Enterprise Resource Planning. Ignore the jargon — it just means the office side of the business. In Carbon, that's where you:

  • Quote a customer and turn the quote into a sales order when they say yes.
  • Raise purchase orders to suppliers for the materials and services you need.
  • Track inventory — what's on the shelf, where, and how much.
  • Roll up cost automatically, so you know what a part really costs to make and whether the price you quoted leaves a profit.

For Acme, this is the layer that took Radiant Aerospace's order for 500 Counterweight Hooks (sales order SO000069, $36,820) and the layer that bought the steel to make them from Ryerson (purchase order PO000057).

MES — the shop floor

MES stands for Manufacturing Execution System. It's the part operators actually use, out on the floor, to do the work. When the office releases a job (a production order to make a specific quantity of a part), the job appears in the MES app. An operator opens it, clocks on to an operation, and records how many good pieces they finished. That's what turns a planned order into real parts — and it's what feeds true labor time back into the cost.

For Acme's hook, the MES side ran three operations in order: Flame Cut, then Sandblasting, then Inspection. Each was clocked and completed on job J000097.

QMS — quality

QMS stands for Quality Management System. This is how you make sure each part is right and can prove it later. In Carbon that means:

  • Inspections — a quality check, often built right into a job as an operation (notice "Inspection" was the last step on the hook).
  • Non-conformances (NCRs) — a record you raise when something is wrong, so it gets reviewed and fixed instead of slipping through.
  • Traceability (also called genealogy) — the linked chain from finished part back to the exact batch of raw material it came from. Because Acme's A36 steel is batch-tracked, Carbon can answer "which heat of steel went into Radiant's hooks?" in seconds.

New to any of these words? Every bolded term — item, method, job, sales order, non-conformance and more — is defined in the glossary. You never have to guess what something means.

One database underneath

The reason the three sides feel like one tool is simple: they sit on one database. A sales order, the job that fulfills it, the purchase order for its material, the inspection that passed it, and the invoice that bills it are all linked records pointing at each other.

Who uses each part

You don't have to learn all of Carbon to be useful in it. Most people live in one or two areas. Here's roughly who touches what at a shop like Acme.

RoleMostly usesDay to day
Estimators & salesERPBuild quotes, price work, convert won quotes into sales orders.
Buyers & purchasingERPRaise purchase orders, chase deliveries, receive material into stock.
Owners & managersERPWatch orders, costs, margins and cash across the whole business.
Machine operatorsMESOpen jobs on the floor, clock onto operations, record finished pieces.
Quality inspectorsQMSRun inspections, raise non-conformances, keep traceability intact.

Because it's all one system, the hand-offs between these roles are automatic. The estimator doesn't email the order to the floor; the order becomes a job the operator can open. The inspector doesn't keep a separate quality binder; the inspection is a step on that same job.

Why Carbon is different

Plenty of software does ERP, MES, or QMS. A few things make Carbon stand apart.

Everything lives together, so there's no re-keying and nothing drifts out of sync. A quote becomes a sales order becomes a job becomes a shipment becomes an invoice — each one inheriting from the last. The walkthrough follows exactly that chain for Acme's hook, from price request to paid invoice, without anyone copying a number by hand.

An API (Application Programming Interface) is a way for other software to read and write your data. Carbon is API-first: every screen you click is doing the same thing a program could do through the API. That means you can connect Carbon to your other tools, build custom reports, or automate the boring parts. More in Using the API.

Carbon ships with a built-in MCP server — a standard doorway that lets an AI assistant safely operate Carbon for you (look things up, draft a quote, check a job's status) in plain language. The same demo data in this manual was pulled through it. See MCP & AI.

Carbon's code is open source — anyone can read it, run it, and extend it. You're not locked into a black box: you can self-host, audit how it works, and shape it to your shop. The integration story continues in Integrating with Carbon.

You don't need to understand the API or AI features to get full value from Carbon. They're there when you're ready. Day one, you can quote, order, make, buy, inspect and invoice entirely by clicking — exactly as the rest of this manual shows.

The one idea to remember

If you take away a single thing: Carbon is one connected system of record for the whole business. ERP runs the office, MES runs the floor, QMS keeps it good — but they're three windows into the same data, not three programs. Information goes in once and follows the work from the first quote to the final payment.
