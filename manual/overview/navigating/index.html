Navigating Carbon

A tour of the app: modules, layout, search and where to find things.

The first time you open Carbon there's a lot on screen — a column of icons down the left, a search box up top, and a big table in the middle. The good news: the whole app is built from a handful of repeating pieces. Learn those pieces once and every screen, from a purchase order to a quality inspection, suddenly feels familiar. This page is the guided tour: what's where, how to jump anywhere in two keystrokes, and how to read the two screens you'll see most — the list and the record.

Before you start. This page assumes you can sign in to Carbon (or you're following along with the live demo). If you haven't yet, your administrator sets you up in Users & permissions. New to the ideas behind Carbon — items, jobs, orders? Read Core concepts alongside this page; here we focus only on driving the interface.

The big picture

Every page in Carbon shares the same frame. A module rail runs down the left edge — that's how you switch between big areas of the business like Sales, Purchasing or Production. Across the top sits the search bar (your fastest way to jump anywhere) plus your account and notifications. Everything else — the list, the record you opened, the form you're filling in — loads in the large content area in the middle.

You don't need every module from day one. Most people live in one or two — a buyer almost never leaves Purchasing; a planner lives in Production. What you can see is also governed by your permissions, so your rail may be shorter than the one above.

Jump anywhere with search (⌘K)

The single most useful habit in Carbon is the command palette. Press ⌘K (on Windows, Ctrl+K) — or click the search bar at the top — and a search box opens over whatever you're doing. Start typing and Carbon searches everything at once: records by number, parts by name, and the app's own navigation.

Type a record number you already know — SO000069, J000097, Q000061 — and press Enter to land straight on it. Type a part name like Counterweight Hook and you'll get the item plus every order and job that touches it. You can also search for screens themselves: typing Suppliers or Work Centers jumps you to that list without ever touching the rail.

⌘K is faster than clicking once you trust it. Think of it the way you'd use a browser's address bar: don't navigate to a thing — just name it.

Lists vs records: the two screens you'll always see

Almost everything you open in Carbon is one of two screens. Understanding the difference is most of what "knowing the app" means.

  • A list (also called a table or index) shows many records of one kind — every job, every customer, every purchase order — one per row. You use it to find, filter and scan.
  • A record (also called a detail view) shows one of those records in full, with all its fields, tabs and related documents. You use it to read or edit a single thing.

You move between them constantly: open a list, click a row, and you're in that record. The flow below is the same everywhere in Carbon.

The list view

A list is a table. Every row is one record; the columns are its most important fields. At the top you'll find a search/filter bar to narrow the rows, and a New button to create one. Status columns use color-coded badges so you can scan the health of dozens of records at a glance.

A few things to know about every list in Carbon:

  • Click a row to open that record. The row number, like SO000069, is also a clickable link.
  • Search and filter at the top narrow the rows — by status, customer, date, and more.
  • Sort by clicking a column header.
  • Columns are yours to arrange. You can show, hide and reorder columns, and Carbon remembers your choice for that list.

The record view

Click SO000069 and you land on its record. A record gathers everything about one thing: its key fields up top, a status badge showing where it stands, and a set of tabs that group related information — and related documents, like the lines on an order or the shipments against it.

The tabs are the key to a record. On this sales order, Details holds the header you see above; Lines holds the parts ordered; Shipments and Invoices hold the documents created from it. Different record types have different tabs — a Job has Operations and Materials, a Part has Manufacturing and Costing — but the pattern is always the same: header on top, related detail in tabs.

Records are linked. From SO000069 you can hop straight to the job that makes it (J000097), the shipment that sent it, or the invoice that billed it (AR000025) — usually by clicking the linked number. This is the heart of how Carbon works: one continuous chain of records, each a click from the next. You can watch the whole chain unfold in the 15-minute walkthrough.

Reading status badges

Carbon uses small colored status badges everywhere — in lists, on records, in search. They're shorthand for "where is this in its lifecycle?" The colors are consistent across the app, so once you learn them you can read any screen at a glance:

Looks likeRoughly meansExample
DraftStarted, not yet finalizedA quote you're still building
ReadyOpen / awaiting actionA job ready for the floor
In ProgressUnderway right nowA job being worked
CompletedDone / active / paidSO000069, AR000025
On HoldPaused on purposeA customer on credit hold
CancelledStopped / rejectedA cancelled order

Each record type has its own exact set of statuses — a purchase order moves through Draft → Needs Approval → To Receive → To Invoice, for instance, while a quote moves Draft → Sent → Accepted. The colors stay consistent even when the words differ, so amber always means "in motion" and green always means "good / done".

Creating a new record

Wherever there's a list, there's a way to add to it. The pattern is always the same:

Go to the right list. Pick the module in the left rail, then the list you want — for a new order, Sales → Sales Orders. Or just press ⌘K and type the list name.

Click New (top-right of the list). Carbon opens a blank record — often as a panel that slides in — and assigns the next number automatically. You don't pick the number; Carbon hands you SO000072 after SO000071.

Fill in the header. Choose the customer or supplier, the location, dates and terms. Required fields are marked; Carbon won't let you save without them.

Save, then add the detail. Once the header is saved the record opens in full, with its tabs. Now add the lines — the parts, quantities and prices — under the Lines tab.

Advance the status when you're ready. A new record starts as a Draft. Actions like Confirm, Release or Send (usually a button near the status) move it forward and trigger the next steps — like creating a job from a confirmed order.

Many records start in Draft and do nothing until you advance them. A purchase order sitting in Draft hasn't been sent to the supplier; a quote in Draft hasn't reached the customer. If something "didn't happen," check its status first — it's the most common surprise for new users.
