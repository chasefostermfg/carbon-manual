Sales orders

Capture demand and drive fulfillment from a sales order.

A sales order (SO) is your customer's commitment to buy: who's ordering, what, how many, when it's due, and at what price. It's the single most important record in fulfillment because everything downstream hangs off it — the job that makes the part, the purchase order that buys the material, the shipment that sends it, and the invoice that bills for it all point back to this one document. Enter the order once and Carbon carries that information everywhere the work goes.

This page follows Acme's real order SO000069 — 500 Counterweight Hooks for Radiant Aerospace, $36,820, Make to Order — from creation to completion.

Where this fits. A sales order usually starts life as a quote the customer accepts. From here, Carbon spawns the job that makes the part, then the order flows on to shipping and the invoice. Quote → Sales Order → Job → Shipment → Invoice.

What a sales order is made of

Every sales order has two parts:

  • A header — the facts that apply to the whole order: the customer, the order date, the payment terms, the shipping location, and the customer's own purchase-order number (their "PO reference").
  • One or more lines — each line is one thing the customer is buying: an item, a quantity, a unit price, a promised date, and a fulfillment method that tells Carbon how you'll deliver it. One order can mix made-to-order parts, stock items, and services on separate lines.

Here's SO000069 as it looks in Carbon once it's complete. Notice the four tabs — every sales order organizes its detail the same way.

The customer PO reference (55557777 here) is the number your customer uses for the order on their end. It's not the same as Carbon's SO000069. Capturing it means the number shows up on the packing slip and invoice, so your customer's accounts-payable team can match your invoice to their purchase order without a phone call.

Create a sales order

There are two ways an order is born: you convert a quote the customer accepted, or you start a fresh order by hand. Converting is by far the most common — it's how SO000069 was created — so start there.

When a customer accepts a quote, you don't re-type anything. Carbon copies the lines, quantities, prices, and the linked manufacturing method straight across, and keeps the two records linked so you can always trace an order back to the price you promised.

Open the accepted quote. Go to Sales → Quotes and open the quote — for Acme that's Q000061. Confirm the line, quantity, and price are what the customer agreed to.

Convert to Sales Order. Click Convert to Order (or Finalize) in the top-right of the quote. Carbon creates a new sales order — SO000069 — and copies every line over, including the Make to Order fulfillment method on the Counterweight Hook line.

Review the header. The customer, currency, and payment terms come from the quote and the customer record. Add or confirm the customer PO reference (55557777) and the ship-from location (Headquarters). Set the order date if it isn't today.

Because the quote line and the sales-order line share the same internal ID, Carbon links them directly. Open SO000069 and you can jump straight back to Q000061 — handy when a customer asks "is this the price you quoted me?"

Not every order comes from a quote — a repeat customer might just email a PO. In that case you create the order directly.

New sales order. Go to Sales → Sales Orders and click New. Pick the customer (Radiant Aerospace). Carbon fills in their default payment terms, currency, and ship-to address automatically.

Fill in the header. Set the order date, the ship-from location, and type in the customer's PO reference. Save the header to get an order number.

Add the lines. Add one line per item the customer is buying — covered next.

Add lines

A line is one thing being sold. To add the Counterweight Hook line, you open the Lines tab and pick the item — Carbon pulls its description, unit of measure, and a default price, then you set the quantity and the promised date.

Each line carries a few fields worth knowing:

FieldOn SO000069What it means
ItemCWH-A36-15The part, material, or service being sold.
Quantity500 EAHow many, in the item's unit of measure (EA = Each).
Unit price$73.14What the customer pays per unit. Line total = qty × price.

Promised date2026-06-26The date you've committed to deliver by.

Acme's hook sells for $73.14 while its internal unit cost is a fraction of a dollar — that gap is your margin, and Carbon shows it on the line so you can see profitability before you ever ship. Adding a shipping cost to the line (Acme added $250) bills the freight to the customer too.

Choose a fulfillment method per line

The fulfillment method is the most important choice on a line. It tells Carbon how this particular item gets delivered — and that decision drives everything that happens next. You set it per line, so one order can fulfill some items from stock and make others to order.

MethodUse it when…What Carbon does
Make to OrderThe item is built specifically for this order.Spawns a job to manufacture it (see below).
Pull from StockYou already keep the finished item on a shelf.Reserves on-hand inventory; no job is created.
Drop ShipA supplier ships straight to your customer.Links a purchase order that delivers to the customer's address.

The Counterweight Hook line on SO000069 is Make to Order — Acme doesn't keep 500 finished hooks on a shelf, so each order is built fresh. That one setting is why a job appears next.

An item's default fulfillment method comes from its replenishment setting — a Make part defaults to Make to Order, a Buy part to Pull from Stock. You can override the default on any individual line.

How a line spawns a job

When you confirm an order with a Make to Order line, Carbon creates a job — a production order that carries its own copy of the item's method (its bill of materials and routing). The job is the bridge from "the customer ordered it" to "the shop floor makes it."

For SO000069, the single line spawned Job J000097:

Notice the link runs both ways: the job knows its sales order (SO000069) and the sales order's Details tab lists the jobs it created. You never lose the thread between "what was sold" and "what's being made."

Carbon won't usually create the job until the order is confirmed. Confirming is your signal that the order is real and the shop should commit material and machine time to it — so review your lines before you confirm.

Promised dates and the due date

The promised date on a line is the date you've told the customer they'll have the goods. Carbon uses it to set the job's due date and to schedule the work backwards from there — so the shop knows when each operation has to start. On SO000069 the promised date is 2026-06-26, which became the due date on job J000097.

Promising a date Carbon can't hit is the most common scheduling mistake. If the routing needs more shop time than the days between today and the promised date, the job will be late before it starts. Check the job's schedule after you confirm — and if material has to be purchased first (Acme bought A36 steel on PO000057), build that lead time into the promise.

Sales order statuses

A sales order moves through a short lifecycle. The status tells you, at a glance, where the order is in fulfillment.

StatusWhat it means
DraftBeing built — lines and prices can still change. Nothing is committed.
ConfirmedThe order is real. Jobs are created for Make-to-Order lines and the work is scheduled.
To ShipGoods are ready (or being made) and waiting to be picked and shipped.
CompletedEvery line is fully shipped and invoiced. SO000069 is here.
CancelledThe order was called off before fulfillment finished.

Status is tracked at the line level too: each line shows how much has been sent and invoiced. SO000069's line shows 500 sent of 500 and 500 invoiced — that's why the whole order rolled up to Completed.

Track shipments and invoices

The remaining two tabs on the sales order are where fulfillment shows up. You don't fill them in directly — they're populated as shipments leave and invoices post — but they're how you answer "did this go out?" and "did we bill for it?"

Every time goods leave against the order, a shipment record appears here, with the quantity sent and the date. SO000069 has one shipment covering all 500 hooks, sent 2026-06-11. Each shipment links to the packing slip your customer receives.

When you bill the order, the sales invoice pulls its lines straight from what shipped — no re-keying — and appears on this tab. SO000069 was billed on invoice AR000025 for $36,820 (the $36,570 of goods plus $250 shipping) and marked Paid the same day.

Radiant Aerospace keeps coming back: the same Counterweight Hook runs on SO000070 (1,500 EA, in progress) and SO000071 (3,000 EA, queued). Each is its own sales order with its own job — but they all point back to the same item and method, so Acme prices and makes them the same way every time.
