Customers

Create customers, contacts, locations and payment terms.

A customer is anyone you sell to. In Carbon, a customer record is the home base for one company you do business with — it holds their people (contacts), their addresses (ship-to and bill-to locations), how and when they pay you (payment terms), and even the part numbers they use for your parts. Once a customer exists, you can quote them, take their orders, ship to them, and invoice them — all without re-typing their details each time.

This page walks you through creating a customer, filling in everything Carbon needs, and reading a customer record. We'll use Acme's real customers throughout, and build out Radiant Aerospace in full — the customer behind the Counterweight Hook order you may have seen in Carbon in 15 minutes.

Where this fits. Customers are the who of selling — the starting point for everything on the sales side. Once a customer exists, you can write them a quote, turn that into a sales order, and eventually bill them. This is the first stop in the Quote → Sales Order → Job → Shipment → Invoice flow.

The customer list

Everything starts on the Customers list, under the Sales module. Each row is one company. At a glance you can see who's active and what kind of business they are. Acme has 16 customers; here are five of them.

Two columns are worth a closer look before you start creating customers — status and type.

Status: where a customer is in your relationship

Every customer has a status that controls whether you can actively sell to them. Carbon ships with five:

StatusWhat it means
LeadA prospect you're quoting but who hasn't bought yet.
ActiveA live customer you can quote, order and invoice.
On HoldTemporarily paused — often for a credit or payment issue.
InactiveNo longer trading, but kept for history.
CancelledThe relationship is closed.

Type: how you group customers

A customer type is a label you use to slice your customers into segments — handy for filtering lists and reporting. Acme groups its customers by industry: Aerospace & Defense, Construction, Dealers, Energy & Power, and Mining. Radiant Aerospace is Aerospace & Defense. Types are optional but make a long customer list far easier to navigate.

Both statuses and types are fully editable lists you set up once for your company, under Settings. Define the segments that match your business before you start adding customers in bulk, so everything lands in the right bucket from day one. See Set up your company.

Create a customer

Let's create Radiant Aerospace from scratch. The only thing Carbon truly requires is a name — but filling in the rest now saves re-keying later.

Open the new-customer form

From the Customers list, click New Customer (top right). Carbon opens a blank customer form. Give it a name — this is the one required field — then set the optional details that make the customer usable everywhere else.

Set the customer ID (or let Carbon do it)

Carbon assigns a readable customer ID automatically using your company's numbering sequence — Radiant's is CUS000003, and you rarely type this yourself. Behind the scenes every customer also gets a permanent internal ID (Radiant's is cust_LxoH7AbowNVjTwGSoqkW1Q); you'll see that form of ID in the URL and in the API, but day to day you just use the name.

Save

Click Save. The customer now exists and appears on the Customers list. You can stop here and add the rest later — but the next sections (contacts, locations, payment terms) are what make a customer ready to quote and ship.

Add contacts

A contact is a person at the customer — a buyer, an engineer, an AP clerk. You attach contacts to a customer so that when you write a quote or a sales order, you can pick who it's for and who to email. A customer can have as many contacts as you need; one is usually marked as the primary point of contact.

Open the Contacts tab

On the customer record, switch to the Contacts tab and click New Contact.

Enter the person's details

Fill in name, email, phone and (optionally) job title. Email matters most — it's what Carbon uses when you send a quote or order PDF, and it's how the customer can be invited to the customer portal to see their orders.

Save and repeat

Save the contact. Add more for anyone else you deal with — for example a separate accounts-payable contact who should receive invoices. When you later create a quote or order for this customer, these contacts appear in a dropdown so you can pick the right recipient.

The buyer's name and email aren't just decoration. On Radiant's real order SO000069, the selected contact is the person Carbon addressed the order confirmation to — and the same contact flowed through to the invoice AR000025. Set contacts up once and they follow the paperwork.

Add ship-to and bill-to locations

A customer location is an address. Carbon treats two address roles specially:

  • Ship-to — where finished goods are delivered. This drives shipments.
  • Bill-to — where invoices are sent. This drives accounting.

Most customers have one of each (often the same address), but a large customer might receive at several plants while all the bills go to one accounts-payable office. You add as many addresses as you need and tag each one with its role.

Open the Locations tab

On the customer record, go to the Locations tab and click New Location.

Enter the address and its role

Type the street address, city, state, postal code and country. Mark whether this address is a ship-to, a bill-to, or both, and set one as the default so Carbon pre-fills it on new orders. Radiant uses a single headquarters address for both roles, which is the most common setup.

Save

Save each address. On every new quote, order and invoice for this customer, Carbon proposes the default ship-to and bill-to — but you can switch to any other address on the customer in one click.

Set payment terms

Payment terms define when a customer is expected to pay an invoice — for example Net 30 means "due 30 days after the invoice date." Setting terms on the customer means every invoice you raise for them inherits the right due date automatically, so your accounting stays consistent without anyone remembering the rule.

Open the Payment tab

On the customer record, go to the Payment tab. Pick the customer's default payment terms from the dropdown (these terms are a reusable list set up in Settings).

Choose the terms and currency

Radiant Aerospace is set to Net 30, billed in USD. You can also set a default tax setting here if the customer is tax-exempt or charged at a specific rate.

Save

Save. From now on, any invoice for this customer defaults to Net 30 — exactly what you'll see on Radiant's real invoice AR000025 (issued and due against the same order).

Terms set on the customer are defaults, not handcuffs. You can override the payment terms on a single order or invoice when a deal calls for it — the customer default just spares you from setting them every time.

Customer-specific part numbers

Here's a small feature that prevents big headaches. Your customer often calls your part by their internal number, not yours. Acme's part is CWH-A36-15 (the Counterweight Hook), but Radiant Aerospace orders it as 730562. Carbon lets you record that mapping once, on the customer, so:

  • When Radiant sends a PO referencing 730562, you instantly know it's CWH-A36-15.
  • When you send Radiant a quote, order or packing slip, Carbon can print their number alongside yours — so the paperwork matches what the customer expects.

This is stored as the customer part number (Carbon's field is customerPartId), and it lives on the link between a customer and one of your items.

You'll see 730562 again on Radiant's real sales order line in Sales orders — Carbon carried the customer part number straight from the customer record onto the order, with no extra typing.

Reading a customer record

Once a customer is built out, its record is a single screen with everything in tabs. Here's Radiant Aerospace as it looks in Acme's system — the Details tab summarizes the company, and the other tabs hold the contacts, locations and payment settings you just created.

That single record is now wired into everything downstream. When you quote, order, ship or invoice Radiant, Carbon pulls their contacts, addresses, terms and part-number mappings automatically — you enter the relationship once and it follows every transaction.

Avoid creating the same company twice. Duplicate customers split a company's history across two records, so quotes, orders and invoices no longer add up in one place. Before adding a customer, search the list — if they're already there (even as a Lead), edit that record rather than making a new one.
