Replenishment: make vs. buy

Reorder policies, lead times and planning parameters that keep stock flowing.

Every item in Carbon has to come from somewhere. A finished part might roll off your shop floor; a sheet of steel arrives on a truck from a supplier. Replenishment is the word Carbon uses for how an item gets here — and getting it right is what stops your shop from running out of steel mid-job or from making 500 of something nobody ordered. This page explains the two settings that drive all of it (make vs. buy, and the fulfillment method on a line), then the planning numbers — lead time, safety stock, reorder point — that let Carbon top up your stock for you.

Where this fits. You've already learned to define an item, give it a method, and decide what counts as material. Replenishment is the last decision on an item: how do we obtain it? That answer flows straight into Jobs (for the things you make) and Purchasing (for the things you buy).

The two questions Carbon asks

Replenishment comes down to two questions, asked at two different moments.

  1. On the item: in general, do we make this or buy this? Carbon stores the answer in a field called the replenishment system.
  2. On each line (a sales order line, a job material, a quote line): for this specific demand, how do we satisfy it — make a fresh batch, pull from the shelf, or order it in? Carbon stores that answer in a field called the method type.

The item-level answer is the default. The line-level answer is the decision Carbon actually acts on, and it can differ line by line. Hold onto that distinction — it's the single thing people trip over.

Step 1 — The replenishment system (make or buy)

The replenishment system lives on the item itself, on the Replenishment area of the item record. It has three values:

  • Make — Carbon expects to produce this item with a job. It must have a method (a BOM + routing). Our hero part, the Counterweight Hook, is Make.
  • Buy — Carbon expects to purchase this item from a supplier on a purchase order. It needs a supplier and a purchase price. The A36 steel plate the hook is cut from is Buy.
  • Make & Buy — both are allowed. Use this for an item you usually make but sometimes buy in (or vice versa). You then pick make-or-buy per line.

Here are the two hero items side by side. Same company, opposite answers.

A quick rule of thumb: if an item has a method, it's almost always Make. If it has a preferred supplier and a purchase price, it's Buy. Carbon won't let a Buy item go onto a job as something-to-produce, and it won't let a Make item with no supplier go onto a purchase order — the replenishment system is what gates those choices.

Step 2 — The method type on a line

When an item shows up as demand — a customer orders it, or a parent job needs it as a component — Carbon has to decide how to satisfy that one line. That choice is the method type, and there are three:

  • Make to Order (MTO) — create a brand-new job tied to this demand. The job exists to fill this order and nothing else. Best for custom or low-volume parts you don't stock.
  • Make to Stock (MTS) — make a batch into inventory ahead of demand, then ship from the shelf. Best for parts you sell repeatedly and want ready to go.
  • Pull from Inventory — don't make or buy anything new for this line; consume what's already on the shelf. This is how components get drawn into a parent job, and how stocked finished goods get shipped.

On the Counterweight Hook's sales order, the line is Make to Order — so Carbon spun up job J000097 the moment the order was confirmed. Inside that job's method, the A36 steel appears as a material line set to Pull from Inventory — Carbon expects to draw the 40 lb per piece from the shelf rather than buy fresh steel for the job itself.

"Pull from Inventory" assumes the inventory is there. Carbon will happily plan to pull 20,000 lb of steel for this job — but if the shelf is short, that shortage becomes new demand for the steel, which (because steel is a Buy item) turns into a suggested purchase order. That's exactly how PO000057 to Ryerson came to exist. Make and buy aren't separate worlds; one feeds the other.

How demand and supply drive planning

Here's the mental model that makes everything else click. Carbon is constantly comparing two columns for every item, at every location:

Demand
What's needed
sales orders, job materials, forecasts, safety stock
Supply
What's coming
on-hand stock, open jobs, open purchase orders

When demand outruns supply, Carbon flags a shortage and proposes the fix that matches the item's replenishment system:

  • A shortage on a Make item becomes a suggested job.
  • A shortage on a Buy item becomes a suggested purchase order.

You review those suggestions in Carbon's planning views and turn the good ones into real jobs and POs. Nothing happens behind your back — Carbon does the arithmetic, you make the call.

Notice every line of demand here is matched by a job that fills it — because the hook is Make to Order, supply is created to order. For a Buy item like the A36 steel, the supply column fills with purchase orders instead.

Step 3 — Set the planning parameters

For a pure Make-to-Order part, planning is simple: an order arrives, a job is made, done. The numbers below matter most for items you stock — raw materials you buy in bulk, and finished goods you make to stock — because Carbon uses them to decide when and how much to replenish before you run dry.

You'll find these on the item's Planning tab, set per location (Headquarters and Texas can have different numbers for the same item).

Pick a reordering policy. This tells Carbon when to suggest a top-up. The common choices are:

  • Manual Reorder — Carbon never auto-suggests; you create jobs and POs yourself.
  • Demand-Based Reorder — Carbon looks ahead over a window of time (a demand window, set to 4 weeks for both hero items), totals up the demand in that window, and suggests enough supply to cover it. This is what both hero items use.
  • Maximum Quantity — keep the shelf topped up toward a target level (a classic min/max).

Set the reorder point and quantities. Depending on the policy, you'll fill in some of:

  • Reorder point — the on-hand level that triggers a refill. Drop below it and Carbon raises a suggestion.
  • Reorder quantity — how much to order each time you do.
  • Minimum / maximum order quantity — floor and ceiling on a single order (a supplier might require a 1,000-lb minimum, for example).
  • Maximum inventory quantity — the ceiling you top up toward under a Maximum Quantity policy.
  • Order multiple — round orders up to a sensible pack size (order in multiples of, say, one full sheet rather than odd pounds).

Set safety stock. This is the buffer you want sitting on the shelf beyond known demand, to absorb surprises. Acme keeps 3,000 LB of A36 steel as safety stock at Headquarters, so Carbon always plans to keep that cushion on hand on top of whatever the jobs need. The hook itself carries no safety stock — it's made to order, so there's nothing to buffer.

Set the lead time. Lead time is how long replenishment takes — days to receive a PO, or days to run a job. Both hero items use a 7-day lead time. Carbon works backwards from your due date by the lead time to tell you the latest day you can place the order or release the job and still be on time.

Set lot sizing and scrap. Lot size controls batching — make/buy in fixed lots (0 means "exactly what's needed", no rounding). Scrap percentage pads quantities for expected loss, so a job that needs 500 good pieces might be sized a little larger. Both hero items use lot size 0 and 0% scrap.

A reorder point is per location, not per company. Setting a reorder point at Headquarters does nothing for Texas. If you stock the same material in two places, give each location its own planning numbers — otherwise one site will quietly run dry while the other is overstocked.

Putting it together: a policy at a glance

Different items, different jobs to do — so different replenishment settings. Here's how a few real Acme items compare, and why each is set the way it is.

Read it as a story: the hook is made fresh for each order; the steel it's cut from is bought in bulk and pulled off the shelf, with a 3,000-lb cushion so a job never stalls waiting for material; the Thermal Exhaust Nozzle is made in large batches into stock because it sells steadily; and nitrile gloves are just bought and consumed. Same machinery, four different replenishment strategies — all driven by those two fields and a handful of planning numbers.

Start simple. You don't need every parameter on day one. Set the replenishment system (make or buy) on every item, set a lead time so dates are realistic, and add safety stock and reorder points only to the items where running out actually hurts. You can always tighten the numbers once you see how stock really moves.
