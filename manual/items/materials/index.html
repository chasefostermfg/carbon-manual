Materials

Raw materials, forms, grades and dimensions for cut-to-size stock.

A material is the raw stock you buy and then cut, machine or form into parts — a steel plate, an aluminum billet, a length of brass bar. In Carbon a material is a special kind of item, built to describe physical stock the way a metals distributor sells it: a substance (what alloy), a form (plate, bar, tube…), a grade, and dimensions. This page explains what makes a material different from a part, how Carbon names and organizes them, and how to create one — using Acme's real steel, stainless, aluminum and Inconel stock as the example throughout.

Where this fits. Materials are the raw stock that feeds your methods and jobs. On the hero thread, the Counterweight Hook (CWH-A36-15) is a Part made from one material — A36 Steel Plate 1″ — which Acme buys from Ryerson on purchase order PO000057. Define the material here, and it's ready to drop into a bill of materials.

Material vs. Part: what's the difference?

Both a part and a material are items, so they share the same plumbing — a unique ID, a unit of measure, costs, inventory tracking, suppliers. The difference is what they describe and how you usually replenish them.

PartMaterial
What it isSomething you make or sell as a finished thingRaw stock you consume to make parts
Identified byA part number you assignSubstance + form + grade + size
Usual sourceMakeBuy
Typical unitEA (each)LB, IN, FT, sheet
Lives in a method asThe thing being built (or a sub-assembly)A consumed input on the bill of materials

The line gets blurry on purpose — a material can be made (some shops melt and pour their own), and a part can be bought. What you pick mostly drives the defaults Carbon shows you. The practical reason to model raw stock as a material rather than a generic part is that Carbon then understands its anatomy: it knows "A36 plate, 1 inch thick, 96×240 sheet" is a single substance in three descriptive pieces, so it can group, search and reorder stock the way your buyers actually think.

If you're unsure whether something is a part, a material, a tool, a consumable or a service, see the item type overview. Materials are the right choice for anything you cut, machine, weld or form from raw stock.

Substances

A substance is the underlying material — the alloy or compound. Carbon ships with 17 system-defined substances, and you can add your own. Acme's catalog draws on six of them — steel, stainless steel, aluminum, brass, Inconel and titanium. A few from Acme's shelves:

Carbon's 17 built-in substances are Aluminum, Brass, Bronze, Copper, Inconel, Magnesium, Monel, Nylon, Plastic, PVC, Rubber, Stainless Steel, Steel, Titanium, Tungsten, Zinc and Zirconium. Need something exotic? You can define a company-specific substance — Acme added Foam for its packaging stock.

The form matters because it shapes how you measure and cut the material: plate is sold by area or weight, bar by length, tube by length and wall thickness.

Grades and dimensions

Grade captures the temper or spec — T6 on aluminum, the alloy designation baked into the name (304 vs 316 stainless, A36 vs 4140 steel). Dimensions capture the physical size: plate thickness and sheet size (96×240 inches), bar diameter, tube wall. In Acme's catalog, grade and dimension live inside the readable ID rather than as separate lookup fields, which keeps the part list scannable.

Reading an Acme material ID. A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240 decodes as: A36 (grade) · STEEL (substance) · PLT (form = plate) · 1″ (thickness) · 96×240 (sheet size in inches). Once you can read the pattern, the whole catalog becomes self-describing.

Acme's material catalog

Here's a real slice of Acme's 19-material catalog, straight from the system. Notice how the naming convention lets the same substance appear in several thicknesses and sheet sizes — each one is its own item because it's physically a different piece of stock to buy, store and cut.

Almost every material is bought in LB (pounds) — the natural unit for metal stock — even when the part it feeds is counted in EA (each). Carbon happily mixes units: the Counterweight Hook's method consumes 40 LB of plate per 1 EA hook. More on units in the glossary.

Cut-to-size stock

Most raw stock isn't consumed whole — you cut a piece off a larger plate or bar. Carbon models this naturally: the material is the full stock item (a 96×240 sheet of 1-inch A36), and each job consumes only the quantity it needs, expressed in the material's unit of measure.

On the hero thread, the Counterweight Hook's method pulls 40 LB of A36 plate per hook. A batch of 500 hooks therefore needs 20,000 LB — not 500 whole sheets. Carbon tracks the running pounds on hand and consumes against it, so a single sheet feeds many parts and you always know how much usable plate is left.

Buying stock in one unit and consuming it in another is common — you might purchase plate by the sheet but consume it by the pound. Carbon supports a purchase unit and an inventory unit with a conversion between them. Acme keeps it simple and uses LB for both on its steel. See purchasing basics for the buy side.

Batch and lot tracking

For materials, where the metal came from often matters as much as how much you have. A batch (or lot, or heat for steel) is a group of stock that arrived together from one supplier shipment. Turning on batch tracking tells Carbon to record a batch number every time the material is received, and to carry that number forward into every part made from it.

Acme's A36 plate has requiresBatchTracking switched on. That single flag means:

At receipt, Carbon asks for (or generates) a batch number when the steel arrives from Ryerson on PO000057. The pounds are added to inventory tagged with that batch.

During the job, when 20,000 LB of plate is issued to job J000097, Carbon records which batch (or batches) was consumed.

Forever after, the finished Counterweight Hooks carry a link back to that exact heat of steel. If Ryerson later reports a problem with that heat, Acme can answer "which orders did that steel ship in?" in seconds.

This chain — supplier shipment → material batch → job → finished part → customer — is what manufacturers call traceability (or genealogy). It's a requirement in aerospace, defense and medical work, and it's exactly why Radiant Aerospace cares that Acme runs batch-tracked steel. Read more in Tracking quality.

Decide on batch (or serial) tracking before you start receiving a material. Switching it on after stock already exists in inventory is messy, because the existing quantity has no batch number attached. For regulated work, turn it on when you first create the material.

Batch vs. serial. Batch tracking groups many units under one number — right for bulk stock like plate and bar where individual pounds are interchangeable. Serial tracking gives every single unit its own number — right for high-value finished parts you must track one-by-one. Materials almost always use batch tracking.

Create a material

Here's how Acme added the 1-inch A36 plate. You'll create materials the same way for steel, stainless, aluminum, brass — anything you cut from raw stock.

Open Items and start a new material. In the Items module, choose New → Material. Picking Material (rather than Part) gives you the substance/form/grade/dimension fields instead of a blank part record.

Pick the substance, form and grade. Choose Steel as the substance and Plate (PLT) as the form from Carbon's built-in lists. Enter the grade — A36 — and the dimensions: 1″ thickness, 96 × 240 inch sheet. Carbon assembles these into a suggested readable ID following your naming pattern (here, A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240).

Set the unit of measure. Choose LB (Pound) — the unit you'll buy and consume the plate in. This is the unit every quantity for this material is expressed in, from purchase orders to bills of materials to on-hand stock.

Set replenishment to Buy. Because Acme buys plate rather than making it, set the replenishment system to Buy. This unlocks the supplier and reorder settings and tells Carbon to suggest a purchase order when stock runs low.

Turn on batch tracking (if needed). For traceable stock like aerospace steel, switch on Requires batch tracking now — before any inventory exists. Acme leaves this on for all its structural metals.

Add the default supplier. On the Suppliers tab, add Ryerson as the source, with the supplier's price (Acme pays $0.25/LB). Now the material is ready to be reordered and to appear on a purchase order like PO000057.

Save. The material is now a first-class item: it can be dropped onto any method, stocked in any location, and bought from any supplier. The hero thread's hook is now buildable.

Reuse, don't duplicate. Before creating a material, search the catalog — Acme already has A36 plate in 1″, 2″, 3″ and 4″ thicknesses. If the exact substance + form + size already exists, use it. Only create a new material when the physical stock is genuinely different.
