Users & permissions

Invite your team, assign employee types and control who can do what.

A real shop has more than one person in it — a salesperson quoting work, a buyer raising purchase orders, operators on the floor, a bookkeeper closing the month. Carbon lets you invite all of them and then control, precisely, what each one can see and change. This page shows you how to invite people by email, choose the right employee type for each, and fine-tune module-level permissions so everyone has exactly the access they need — and nothing more.

Before you start. This is a foundation step, best done right after you set up your company and before you migrate your data. You'll need to be an admin to invite people. If you set up the company yourself, you already are one — in Acme's account that's Chase Foster (chase@carbon.ms).

The two ideas: who you are, and what you can touch

Access in Carbon comes from two layers that work together. Keep them separate in your head and the whole thing gets simple.

  • A user is one login — one person, one email address. Inviting someone creates their user account.
  • An employee type (Carbon's word for a role) is a named bundle of permissions you attach to a user. Instead of setting access person-by-person, you define a type once — say Sales — and assign it to everyone who sells.
  • Permissions are the actual switches. They're granted per module (Sales, Purchasing, Items, Production, and so on) and, within each module, per action: view, create, update, and delete.

So the flow is always the same: invite the user, give them an employee type, and let that type's permissions decide what they can do in each module. Let's walk through it.

Invite your team

You add people by sending an email invitation. They click the link, set a password, and land in your company with whatever access their employee type grants.

Open Settings → Users. From the left navigation, go to Settings, then Users. This is the list of everyone in your company and the type assigned to each. Acme's looks like this.

Click Invite and enter their email. Type one or more email addresses — you can invite several people at once by separating addresses with a comma. Pick the employee type they should get (you can change it later), then send.

They accept and set a password. Carbon emails the invite. When the person clicks the link, they create a password and they're in — with exactly the access their type defines. Until they accept, their status shows as Invited rather than Active.

You can deactivate a user instead of deleting them — handy when someone leaves but you want to keep their name on the records they created (like the way Chase Foster stays attached to purchase order PO000057 and sales order SO000069 forever). Deactivating frees up the seat without erasing history.

Choose an employee type (the role)

An employee type is the role you hand someone. It carries a default set of module permissions so you don't configure each person from scratch. A new Carbon company starts with a few sensible types, and you can add your own to match how your shop is organized.

The one type that always exists and that you can't lock yourself out of is Administrator: it can reach every module and the Settings area where users and types are managed. Keep at least one.

Suzie's view, in practice

When you give Suzie Sells the Sales type, Carbon opens up the selling side of the app for her — customers, quotes and sales orders — and leaves the rest read-only or hidden. She can build a quote like Q000061 and turn it into an order, but she can't change a supplier price or post to the ledger. That's the role doing its job.

Employee types also pair naturally with departments. Acme groups people into Sales, Marketing, and Sales & Marketing departments. Departments don't grant access on their own — they're for organizing people and reporting — but they make a growing team easier to navigate.

Set module-level permissions

This is the heart of access control. Within an employee type, you turn on four switches for each module: View, Create, Update, and Delete. They stack the way you'd expect — you can't create something you can't view, and Delete is the one you hand out most sparingly.

Open the type and find the module grid. From Settings → Employee Types, click a type (say Sales) and open its Permissions tab. You'll see one row per module with four checkboxes each.

Tick the actions this role needs. For a salesperson, you'd give full access to Sales, let them view Items so they can pick parts onto a quote, and leave Purchasing, Production and Accounting off entirely.

Save. The change applies to everyone with that type immediately — there's no need to re-edit each person. Next time Suzie loads the app, Purchasing simply isn't there.

Read a column to understand one role; read a row to see who can touch one module. Notice the Accountant can view Sales and Purchasing (they need to see the orders behind an invoice) but only has full control over Accounting — and that only the Administrator reaches Settings, where users and types live.

Grant least privilege. Give each person the minimum access their job needs, not the most. It's tempting to make everyone an Administrator to avoid "access denied" friction — don't. Over-broad permissions are how a salesperson accidentally edits a supplier price, or an operator deletes a sales order. Start narrow, then add a permission when someone actually hits a wall. Delete in particular should be rare: most roles never need it, because Carbon prefers to cancel or deactivate records rather than erase the history other records depend on.

A note on AI and API access

Carbon is API-first and ships with a built-in MCP server, so an AI assistant can read and act in your company on your behalf. That access runs through the same permission system you just set up — an AI agent or an API key inherits the access of the user it acts as. The least-privilege rule matters even more here: scope a key to exactly the modules a script or assistant needs. There's a whole section on this in Developing with Carbon.
