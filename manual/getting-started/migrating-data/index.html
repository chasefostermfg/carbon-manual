Migrating your data

Bring your items, customers and suppliers into Carbon.

If you're moving to Carbon from spreadsheets or another system, you don't have to retype everything by hand. This page shows you what to bring in, in what order, and how — using Carbon's built-in CSV importers. The golden rule of any migration is simple: import the things other records depend on first. Get the order right and the rest falls into place.

We'll use Acme Inc.'s own data as the worked example throughout — the same parts, customers and suppliers you'll see everywhere else in this manual.

Before you start. This page assumes your company is already set up (Set up your company) and that you've added the people who'll be doing the work (Users & permissions). Migration is the last step of getting started — once your data is in, you're ready to define and use items for real.

Why order matters

Carbon records are linked. An item points to a unit of measure. A piece of opening inventory points to an item and a location. A bill of materials points to the parent item and every child item. An open sales order points to a customer and an item.

So if you import opening stock before the item exists, Carbon has nothing to attach it to and the row fails. Import items before their units of measure exist, and the unitOfMeasureCode column has nothing to match. The fix is to load your data in dependency order — each layer building on the one below it.

You don't have to migrate history. Most shops bring in their master data (items, customers, suppliers) plus a snapshot of opening inventory and any open orders, and leave closed jobs and paid invoices in the old system. A clean start beats a perfect copy.

Step 1 — Foundations: units of measure & locations

A unit of measure (UoM) is how you count an item — EA for "each", LB for pounds, IN for inches. A location is a physical site that holds stock. These are the codes every later import leans on, so they go in first. Carbon ships with the common ones already defined, so usually you're just confirming they exist rather than creating them.

Decide your UoM and location codes now and never change them later. Every imported row, every BOM line and every stock balance refers to these codes by exact text — rename EA to ea afterward and you'll orphan thousands of references. Pick the casing once and stick with it.

Step 2 — Items (parts, materials, consumables, tools)

Items are the heart of Carbon — a part you make, a material you buy, a consumable, a tool. Almost everything else points at an item, so this is the biggest and most important import. Get your spreadsheet clean here and the rest of the migration is easy.

Each row becomes one item. The columns map to real Carbon fields:

Here's a slice of Acme's items file — real parts and materials from the dossier. Notice the two key decisions on every row: its itemType (is it a part, a raw material, a tool?) and its replenishmentSystem (do you make it or buy it?).

Keep your readableId values consistent — Acme uses patterns like [abbrev]-[material]-[rev] for parts (CWH-A36-15) and [alloy]-[substance]-[form]-[thickness]".[dimension] for materials (A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240). A predictable scheme makes parts findable and your BOM import in Step 5 far easier to wire up.

Step 3 — Customers & suppliers

With items in place, bring in the businesses you sell to and buy from. Customers are needed before any quote, sales order or invoice; suppliers before any purchase order. Each is its own import, but the workflow is identical.

Customer and supplier types (like "Aerospace & Defense" or "Metals and Raw Materials") and payment terms (like "Net 30") are short lookup lists. If your CSV uses a type Carbon doesn't recognize yet, create it once under Settings before importing — then every row that references it lands cleanly. More in Customers and Suppliers.

Step 4 — Opening inventory

Now that items and locations both exist, you can record what's physically on your shelves today — your opening balances. Each row says: this item, this quantity, at this location (and, if you track bins, which shelf).

Carbon turns each row into an inventory adjustment that sets the item's on-hand quantity. After the import, every part with stock shows a real on-hand figure you can build jobs against.

Import opening inventory once, ideally on go-live day, and pick a clear cutover moment. If you import balances and then keep transacting in your old system, the two drift apart fast. Freeze the old system, count, import, and run forward in Carbon from there.

Step 5 — BOMs & methods

A method is how a made item is built: its bill of materials (what it's made of) plus its routing (the operations that make it). Methods only make sense once the parent and all the child items already exist — which is exactly why they come after Step 2.

For a made-to-order part like the Counterweight Hook, the method is short: one material, three operations. Here's how that recipe looks once it's in Carbon.

To import methods by CSV, you typically prepare two related files and load them in order:

BOM lines — one row per material, linking a parent readableId to a child readableId with a quantity. For the hook: parent CWH-A36-15, child A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240, qty 40 LB.

Routing operations — one row per operation, with its sequence, the process (Flame Cut, Sandblasting, Quality Inspection) and the work center it runs at (Burn Table — PA2, Sand Blasting Room, Inspection Station), plus times like 45 minutes per piece.

Carbon stitches the two together into the parent item's method. From then on, any job for that part copies this recipe automatically.

Multi-level assemblies (a part made of sub-assemblies that are themselves made) import the same way — just make sure every sub-assembly already exists as an item, and import the deepest level's BOM first so each parent can find its children.

Step 6 — Open orders

Last, bring in the work that's still in flight: open quotes, sales orders awaiting fulfillment, and purchase orders awaiting receipt. These reference items, customers and suppliers, so by now everything they point at already exists.

These two rows mirror Acme's real open work — the follow-on Counterweight Hook orders (SO000070 and SO000071) that come right after the hero order SO000069. Once they're in, Carbon can plan jobs and purchasing against them just like any natively created order.

The CSV import workflow

Every importer in Carbon works the same way, so once you've done one you've done them all. The files in a typical migration look like this — loaded top to bottom:

01-units-of-measure.csv
01-locations.csv
02-items.csv
03-customers.csv
03-suppliers.csv
04-opening-inventory.csv
05-bom-lines.csv
05-routing-operations.csv
06-open-sales-orders.csv
06-open-purchase-orders.csv

To load any one of them:

Open the importer. Go to the module that owns the data — Items, Sales, Purchasing, Inventory — and choose Import. Carbon offers a template CSV with the exact column headers it expects; download it and pour your data into those columns.

Upload the file. Drop your .csv in. Carbon reads the header row and previews the first few rows so you can confirm it parsed the way you expected.

Map columns to Carbon fields. For each of your columns, pick the Carbon field it feeds — your "Part No." to readableId, your "UoM" to unitOfMeasureCode, your "Make/Buy" to replenishmentSystem. If your headers already match Carbon's template, the mapping is auto-filled.

Validate. Carbon checks every row before changing anything — flagging blank required fields, a unitOfMeasureCode that doesn't exist yet, or a duplicate readableId. Fix the flagged rows in your spreadsheet and re-upload.

Import. When the preview is clean, confirm. Carbon creates the records and shows a summary — how many created, how many skipped. Spot-check a few against the app, then move on to the next file in the sequence.

Always run a small test batch first — five or ten rows — before importing thousands. A tiny run surfaces a wrong column mapping or a bad UoM code in seconds, instead of after you've created 5,000 mis-mapped parts. Carbon validates before writing, but a real test import is the surest check.

Large or repeatable migrations: use the API

Carbon is API-first — every screen in the app is backed by a documented API, and anything you can type in, you can script. For a big migration, a one-time data move from a legacy ERP, or a sync you'll run more than once, scripting it against the API is far more reliable than hand-mapping CSVs over and over. You control the order, you can re-run safely, and you get a clean log of exactly what was created. See Using the API to get started.

The same dependency order applies whether you click or script: create units and locations, then items, then customers and suppliers, then balances, then methods, then open orders. The API just lets you automate each layer. Because Carbon also ships a built-in MCP server, you can even have an AI assistant drive parts of the migration for you — reading your old data and creating the Carbon records in order.
