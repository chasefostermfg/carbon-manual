Advanced purchasing

Outside processing, RFQs, drop-ship and the supplier portal.

Purchasing basics covered the everyday loop: raise a purchase order, send it, receive the goods. This page is for the trickier buying jobs that real shops run into — sending a half-finished part out to a specialist for a coating, getting competing prices before you commit, shipping straight from your supplier to your customer, and letting Carbon tell you what to buy before a shortage stalls a job. None of these are exotic; you'll likely use all of them within your first month.

Where this fits. Everything here sits inside the buying half of the flow — Quote → Sales Order → Job → Purchasing → Shipment → Invoice. Make sure you're comfortable with Purchasing basics and Suppliers first — this page builds on both.

Outside processing

Sometimes a step in making a part isn't something you do in-house. A part comes off your saw and CNC mill in great shape, but it still needs anodizing or painting — and you don't own an anodizing line. So you send the parts to a specialist supplier, they perform the service, and they ship the parts back to you to finish. That round trip is called outside processing (also "outside service" or "subcontracting").

The key idea in Carbon: outside processing is just an operation in a part's routing, exactly like Flame Cut or Inspection — except its type is Outside instead of Inside. An Inside operation runs at one of your own work centers and costs you shop time. An Outside operation runs at a supplier, and its cost is what the supplier charges you. When the job reaches that operation, Carbon helps you raise a purchase order for the service and tracks the parts while they're off-site.

Acme uses two outside-processing suppliers, both classed as Subcontractors and Service Providers:

Allen's AnodizingAnodizingSubcontractors and Service Providers
Zebra PaintersPaintingSubcontractors and Service Providers

How an outside operation sits in a routing

Picture a part — say an aluminum bracket — that's milled in-house and then anodized at Allen's Anodizing before a final inspection. Its method tree mixes inside and outside steps in sequence:

Operation 20 has no work center, no labor minutes, and no machine time. Instead it points at a supplier and carries a price. Compare that to Acme's hero part, the Counterweight Hook — all three of its operations (Flame Cut, Sandblasting, Inspection) are Inside, so the whole part is made under Acme's own roof.

Set up and run an outside operation

Add the operation to the routing and set its type to Outside. Open the part's Manufacturing tab, add an operation where the service belongs in the sequence, and choose Outside as the operation type. (You can do the same on a one-off job without changing the part — handy for a rush order.)

Point it at a supplier and a service item. Pick the supplier who performs the work — Allen's Anodizing — and link the operation to a Service item if you track services as items. Enter what the supplier charges (per piece or per lot). That price becomes part of the part's rolled-up cost, just like in-house labor does.

Release the job. When you release the job, the outside operation appears in its operation list with status Todo, sitting in sequence between the inside steps before and after it.

Raise a purchase order for the service. When production reaches the outside step, create a purchase order to the supplier for the service. Because the operation is linked to the job, the PO is tied back to it — Carbon knows these parts left the building for anodizing, and which job they belong to.

Ship the parts out, then receive them back. Send the work-in-progress parts to the supplier. When they come back finished, record a receipt against that PO. Receiving the service marks the outside operation complete, and the job moves on to the next step — here, final Inspection.

One supplier can do many things. A subcontractor like Sendcutsend cuts and finishes parts, so it can appear on outside operations for several different services. Set the supplier's supplier type to Subcontractors and Service Providers so it's easy to find when you're building a routing.

Outside parts are still your inventory. While parts are at the anodizer, they haven't been scrapped or shipped to a customer — they're work-in-progress that's temporarily off-site. Don't close the job or promise the parts as finished until you've received them back and the outside operation reads complete. Otherwise your job costs and on-hand counts will be wrong.

Supplier quotes (RFQs)

Before you buy something expensive or new, you usually want more than one price. In Carbon you do that with a Request for Quote (RFQ): you describe what you want to buy, send it to several suppliers, collect their supplier quotes back, and compare them. When you pick a winner, you convert the chosen quote into a purchase order — no re-typing.

A supplier quote is the buying-side mirror of the sales quote you send customers. Carbon creates one for each supplier you ask, so a single RFQ can spin off several supplier quotes you track side by side.

Say Acme needs a big lot of 1-inch A36 steel plate and wants to shop it around. Acme's metals suppliers all carry it, so the RFQ goes to four of them at once:

Olympic SteelMetals and Raw Materials—

Send an RFQ and compare the replies

Create the RFQ and add what you want priced. In the Purchasing module, create a new RFQ and add a line for each item you want quoted — here, one line for A36 Steel Plate 1″ (A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240) at the quantity you're planning to buy. You can add notes, target dates, and attachments (drawings, specs) per line.

Choose the suppliers and send. Add the four suppliers above as recipients. Carbon generates a supplier quote for each one and sends a request — either by email or through the supplier portal, so suppliers can type their prices in directly.

Collect the replies. As suppliers respond, their prices land back on each supplier quote. You'll see quotes come in at different prices, lead times, and minimum quantities. The list view shows you which ones are still outstanding.

Compare side by side. Open the comparison view for the RFQ. Carbon lines the suppliers up against each other so the cheapest price, the best lead time, and any quantity breaks jump out. You're not just chasing the lowest number — a slightly higher price that arrives a week sooner can be the right call when a job is waiting.

Convert the winner to a purchase order. Pick the quote you want and convert it to a purchase order in one click. The supplier, item, price, and quantity carry straight over — and the PO stays linked to the quote, so you always know why you paid what you paid. That's exactly how Acme's PO000057 to Ryerson came to be: A36 plate at $0.25/LB.

Keep the prices for next time. A supplier quote can feed a supplier's price list for that item, so the next time you buy it Carbon already knows the price and lead time — no RFQ needed unless you want to re-shop it.

Drop-ship

Normally goods you buy come to you — you receive them, then ship from your own dock. But sometimes it makes more sense for the supplier to ship straight to your customer. Maybe you're reselling a finished item, or the part is bulky and your shop is just a middleman. That shortcut is a drop-shipment: you still buy it and you still bill your customer, but the goods never touch your building.

In Carbon, drop-ship is a flag that links a sales order and a purchase order together. Acme's hero order SO000069 is a normal (non-drop-ship) order — its dropShipment flag is false, because Acme makes those hooks in-house and ships them itself. A drop-ship order sets that flag to true instead.

Set up a drop-ship

Mark the sales order line as drop-ship. On the sales order, turn on drop-shipment for the line. This tells Carbon not to expect the part to come out of your own inventory.

Generate the linked purchase order. Carbon creates a purchase order to your chosen supplier for that line, with the ship-to set to your customer's address rather than your own warehouse. Confirm the supplier, price, and quantity.

Send the PO; the supplier ships your customer. The supplier fulfills the order directly to the customer's door. Nothing arrives at your shop, so there's no receipt to do and no inventory movement on your side.

Invoice the customer as usual. You still bill your customer from the sales order, and you still pay the supplier from the purchase order. Your margin is the difference — Carbon tracks both sides even though the goods never passed through your hands.

Drop-ship skips your inventory on purpose. Because the part never comes through your dock, it never appears in your on-hand stock — by design. Only use drop-ship for lines you genuinely want fulfilled supplier-to-customer. If you want the goods first (to inspect, kit, or combine with other items), buy it normally and ship it yourself.

The supplier portal

How do suppliers actually reply to an RFQ or confirm a PO? You don't want to chase prices over email and re-key them by hand. Carbon gives each supplier a portal — a secure web link where they can see what you've sent them and respond online, without needing a Carbon login of their own.

When you send a supplier quote or purchase order through the portal, Carbon includes a private link. The supplier clicks it and can:

DocumentWhat the supplier can do
Supplier quote / RFQEnter unit prices, lead times, minimum quantities, and notes — then submit
Purchase orderReview and acknowledge the order, confirm dates, or flag a problem

Whatever the supplier types lands straight on the matching record in your Carbon — no copy-paste, no transcription mistakes. Each request carries its own link, so a supplier only ever sees the documents you sent them.

No account, no friction. The whole point of the portal is to make it easy for suppliers to respond — especially small shops like an anodizer or painter who'll never log in to a full ERP. The link does the work. The faster they can reply, the faster your RFQ comparison fills in.

Purchasing planning

So far you've been creating purchase orders by hand. But how do you know what to buy in the first place? On a busy shop floor, the answer is purchasing planning — Carbon watches your demand and your stock, works out where you're going to run short, and hands you a list of buy suggestions you can turn into purchase orders.

Demand comes from two places:

  • Jobs. When you release a job, its method lists the materials it needs. If you don't have enough on hand, that's a shortage.
  • MRP / replenishment. For stocked items with reorder points (see Replenishment), Carbon flags anything that's dipped below its minimum.

This is exactly how Acme's hero purchase order was born. Job J000097 needed 40 LB of A36 steel plate per piece — 20,000 LB for the batch of 500 — and Acme didn't have that much in stock. Carbon flagged the shortage, and that suggestion became purchase order PO000057 to Ryerson.

Turn suggestions into purchase orders

Open the planning view. In the Purchasing module, open purchasing planning. Carbon lists every item where projected demand is about to outrun supply, the quantity it suggests buying, and what's driving the need (a specific job, or a reorder point).

Review and adjust. You're in control — bump a quantity to hit a price break, change the suggested supplier, or skip a line you'll handle another way. Carbon's number is a starting point, not a command.

Generate the purchase orders. Select the lines you want and let Carbon create the POs. It groups lines by supplier, so one click can produce several tidy orders — all the Ryerson items on one PO, all the Fastenal items on another.

Approve and send. The new POs land in Draft or Needs Approval (just like PO000057 did). From there it's the normal purchasing basics loop — approve, send, receive.

Planning closes the loop. This is the moment the buying side connects back to production: a job creates demand, planning surfaces the shortage, a PO fills it, the receipt restocks the shelf, and the job pulls the material it needs. Run planning regularly and shortages stop sneaking up on you.
