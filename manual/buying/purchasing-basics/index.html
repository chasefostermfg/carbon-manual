Purchasing basics

Purchase orders, receiving and supplier quotes.

When a job needs material you don't have on the shelf, you buy it. In Carbon you do that with a purchase order (PO): a document that tells a supplier exactly what you want, how much, at what price, and when you need it. This page walks you through raising one, getting it approved, sending it, and — the part that actually puts material on your shelf — receiving the goods against it.

We'll follow a real example from Acme: PO000057, a purchase order to Ryerson (Acme's steel supplier) for the A36 Steel Plate that goes into the Counterweight Hooks on job J000097. Each hook eats 40 lb of plate, and the batch of 500 needs 20,000 lb — far more than Acme keeps in stock — so Carbon flagged the shortage and Acme bought the steel.

Where this fits. Buying sits in the middle of the end-to-end flow. A job (or low stock) creates a need for material → you raise a purchase order here → you receive the goods, which adds them to inventory → the material is consumed by the job, and the order eventually gets billed by an accounts-payable invoice. This is the buy-side mirror of the sell-side flow you saw in Carbon in 15 minutes.

The shape of a purchase order

A purchase order has two parts, and they map to the tabs you'll see on the record:

  • Header (the Details tab) — who you're buying from, the order date, where it's delivered, the currency, and the payment terms. One supplier per PO.
  • Lines (the Lines tab) — one row per thing you're buying. Each line names an item, a quantity, a unit price, and a required date (when you need it on site). Line totals add up to the order total.

A Delivery tab holds the ship-to location and any delivery notes. Here is Acme's finished PO000057 so you can see the whole record before we build one:

Get a price first (the supplier quote). Before committing, you can ask one or more suppliers "what would this cost?" with a supplier quote (Carbon calls it a request for quote, or RFQ). You send the item and quantity, suppliers send back a price and lead time, and you turn the best response into a purchase order without re-typing the lines. Acme knows A36 plate runs about $0.25/LB at Ryerson because that price came from earlier quotes and orders — Carbon remembers a supplier's last price on an item and pre-fills it for you.

Create the purchase order

You can start a PO three ways: by hand, from a job's material shortage, or straight from a supplier quote. We'll do it by hand so you see every field, then point out the shortcuts.

Open Purchasing and start a new order

Go to the Purchasing module in the left rail and choose Purchase Orders. Click New Purchase Order. Carbon assigns the next number automatically — Acme's became PO000057 — and opens it in Draft status. Nothing is committed yet; a draft is just a work-in-progress you can edit or delete freely.

Pick the supplier

Choose the supplier you're buying from. Acme selects Ryerson. Picking the supplier does a lot of quiet work: Carbon fills in that supplier's currency (USD), default payment terms (Net 30), and the contact and address on file, so you don't re-enter them.

If the supplier doesn't exist yet, create it first — see Suppliers. A PO must point at a real supplier record, because that's where the payment terms, addresses, and the supplier's prices live.

Set the header dates and delivery location

On the Details tab, confirm the order date (2026-06-12 for Acme) and pick the deliver-to location — Acme ships to Headquarters (loc_CZUGEF…), the same location the job runs at. The payment terms carried over from the supplier; leave Net 30 or override it for this order.

Add the lines

The lines are where you say what you're buying. Each line is one item at one price.

Add a line and choose the item

On the Lines tab, click Add Line. Set the line type to Part (Carbon uses "Part" as the generic line type for any stocked item, including materials), then search for the item. Acme picks A36 Steel Plate 1" — readable ID A36-STEEL-PLT-1".96x240. Carbon pulls in the description and the purchase unit of measure (LB, pounds) from the item record.

Enter quantity, unit price and required date

Fill in the three numbers that matter:

  • Quantity — how much to buy. Acme orders 54,001 LB (a round buy of plate that covers this job's 20,000 lb need and tops up stock for the next ones).
  • Unit price — what the supplier charges per unit. Ryerson's price is $0.25/LB. Carbon pre-fills the supplier's last known price, so often you just confirm it.
  • Required date — the date you need it on site. Acme sets 2026-06-21. This drives Carbon's planning and tells you which lines are at risk of being late.

Carbon multiplies quantity × unit price into the extended price automatically: 54,001 × $0.25 = $13,500.25.

Review the line total

Your single line now reads like this. Carbon rolls the line extended prices up into the order total in the header.

The shortcut: buy straight from a job. Acme didn't really type this line from scratch. When job J000097 was planned, Carbon saw it needed 20,000 lb of A36 plate that wasn't in stock and surfaced a shortage. From there you can generate the purchase order with the item, quantity, and supplier already filled in — see Jobs and Advanced purchasing. Buying and making then run in parallel: the steel is on its way while the shop gets set up.

Approve it and send it

A draft PO commits Acme to nothing. Two steps turn it into a real order: approval, then sending.

Submit for approval

When the order is ready, you submit it for approval. Carbon moves it from Draft to Needs Approval — exactly where PO000057 sits in the screenshot above. This is a control: spending above a threshold can require a manager's sign-off before the order goes out, so nobody accidentally commits the company to a five-figure steel buy. Who needs to approve what is configured in company settings — see Users & permissions.

The PO status walks through these stages as it progresses:

StatusWhat it means
DraftBeing built. Editable, not yet committed.
Needs ApprovalSubmitted; waiting for sign-off.
To ReceiveApproved and sent; goods are on the way.
To InvoiceGoods received; waiting on the supplier's bill.
CompletedReceived and invoiced in full. Closed.

Approve

An authorized approver opens the order, checks the lines and total, and approves it. Approval is what unlocks sending. If something's wrong, they can reject it back to Draft for edits instead.

Send it to the supplier

Once approved, click Send. Carbon generates a clean PO document (PDF) and emails it to the supplier's contact on file — for Acme, that's Ryerson's contact — using the PO number as the reference. The order moves to To Receive: Carbon is now waiting for the steel to show up. You can also download the PDF to send yourself, or share a supplier portal link.

Sending is not receiving. Sending the PO tells the supplier what you want; it does not add anything to your inventory. Material only lands on your shelf when you receive it against the PO — that's the next step, and it's the one beginners most often forget.

Receive the goods

This is the step that actually changes your stock. When the truck arrives, you create a receipt against the purchase order. Receiving does three things at once:

  1. Adds the material to inventory at the deliver-to location, so it's available for the job to consume.
  2. Records how much arrived on each PO line (quantityReceived), so Carbon knows what's still outstanding if the supplier ships short or in parts.
  3. For batch-tracked items, records the lot — the supplier's batch or heat number — so you keep full traceability from raw steel to finished part.

A36 Steel Plate is batch-tracked (requiresBatchTracking: true), so the receipt will ask you for a lot number. That's not bureaucracy: months later, if a hook ever fails inspection, Acme can trace it back to the exact heat of steel it came from — and find every other part made from that same batch.

Start a receipt from the PO

Open PO000057 and choose Receive (you can also start from the Inventory → Receipts list and pick the PO). Carbon opens a new receipt and pre-loads the open lines with the quantity still outstanding — here, the full 54,001 LB, since nothing has been received yet.

Confirm what actually arrived

Count what came off the truck and enter the received quantity per line. If the supplier shipped everything, accept the suggested amount. If they shipped part of it, enter the smaller number — Carbon leaves the remainder open on the PO so you can receive the rest later. Pick the location and shelf/bin the material goes to; Acme's A36 plate lives in bin R02-B01-01 at Headquarters.

Enter the batch (lot) number

Because A36 plate is batch-tracked, Carbon requires a lot number for the quantity you're receiving. Type the supplier's heat or batch number from the mill certificate (the steel's paperwork). This lot is now permanently linked to this material — when the job pulls the plate, Carbon records which lot it consumed.

The lot number shown (HEAT-A36-0612) is illustrative — you enter whatever batch identifier the supplier's mill certificate gives you. Items that aren't batch- or serial-tracked (most consumables and hardware) skip this step entirely.

Post the receipt

Click Post. At that moment Carbon:

  • Increases on-hand stock of A36 plate at Headquarters by the received quantity.
  • Stamps the PO line's quantityReceived and flips the order to To Receive (still open if partial) or onward toward To Invoice once everything's in.
  • Posts the inventory value to your books at the purchase cost, so accounting reflects the new asset.

The steel is now real, on the shelf, and ready for job J000097 to consume.

What happens next

With the material received, the loop closes on its own:

Loading diagram...
  • The job consumes the A36 plate as it runs, drawing down the stock you just received and carrying the lot number forward into the finished hooks.
  • The supplier sends a bill, which you match to the PO and receipt as an accounts-payable invoice. Three-way matching (PO ↔ receipt ↔ bill) is what lets you catch a supplier overcharging before you pay.

Receiving is the hinge between buying and inventory. Everything before the receipt is paperwork; the receipt is the moment material becomes real stock you can use and value. For the full picture of stock, shelves, bins and receipts, see Locations & stock and Shipping & receiving.
