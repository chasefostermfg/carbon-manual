Tracking quality

Non-conformances, inspections, corrective actions and full traceability.

Quality is the part of Carbon that keeps bad parts from leaving the building — and lets you prove it later. This page covers the four pieces of Carbon's QMS (Quality Management System, the glossary term for the tools that control and document quality): inspections baked into a job, non-conformances (NCRs) and the corrective actions that follow them, gauges and their calibration schedule, and traceability — how batch and serial tracking let you answer "which heat of A36 steel went into Radiant's order?" months after the job shipped.

Where this fits. Quality runs alongside the whole flow, not after it. The hero job J000097 ends with an Inspection operation; the steel on PO000057 is batch-tracked when it's received; and if anything goes wrong, an NCR ties it all together. You're reading the chapter between the shop floor making parts and the warehouse shipping them.

You don't have to use all four to start. Many shops begin with a single inspection operation on their most important part and grow from there.

Inspections: a quality gate inside the job

In Carbon an inspection isn't a separate module — it's just an operation in a routing, run at a work center you've set aside for checking parts. The hero Counterweight Hook proves the point. Its method has three operations, and the last one is the gate:

Operation 30 runs at the Inspection Station work center using the Quality Inspection process, and it's allotted 10 minutes per piece. Because the routing is After Previous, no hook reaches inspection until it's been cut and sandblasted — Carbon enforces the order.

How an inspection runs on the shop floor

An inspector clocks onto operation 30. In the MES app, the inspector opens job J000097, taps the Inspection operation, and clocks on — exactly like any other operation. Carbon already knows which procedure applies (Acme's Quality Inspection, version 1, is an Active standard work instruction).

They check parts against the spec. Using calibrated gauges — a height gauge, calipers, a micrometer — they measure features and compare to the drawing. The procedure spells out what to check and how.

They record good and scrap quantities. Good pieces get reported as quantity complete; anything that fails gets reported as scrap with a reason. On J000097 all 500 pieces passed, so quantity complete rolled up to 500 with zero scrap.

A failure becomes an NCR. If parts fail, the inspector raises a non-conformance right from the operation (covered next). The job can be held until the issue is resolved, so bad parts can't be picked and shipped by mistake.

Want first-article or incoming inspection instead of a final gate? Put an inspection operation first in the routing, or inspect material the moment it's received. The mechanism is identical — it's just an operation at the Inspection Station — only the position changes.

Non-conformances (NCRs): recording what went wrong

A non-conformance report — an NCR, sometimes called an issue in Carbon — is the record you open when something doesn't meet spec: a mismeasured part, a bad batch of steel, a customer complaint. It captures what happened, how bad it is, what you did about it, and how you proved the fix worked. That paper trail is what auditors and customers like Radiant Aerospace want to see.

Classify it: NCR types

Carbon ships Acme with ten NCR types so every issue is filed under a cause you can report on later. Filing by type is how you spot patterns — for example, three "Material Issue" NCRs in a month all pointing back to one supplier.

Match the response to the severity: workflows

Not every problem deserves the same paperwork. A scratched part and a customer recall are worlds apart, so Carbon attaches a workflow to each NCR. A workflow is a named playbook — it sets a priority, lists the required actions you must complete before closing, and can require sign-off from a review board. Acme uses three, drawn straight from standard quality practice:

Reading those left to right: 5 Why 2 How is the quick investigation for a minor issue. CAPA — Corrective and Preventive Action — adds a documented fix and a step to stop it recurring. 8D is the heavyweight, eight-discipline response reserved for critical problems; it even requires MRB (Material Review Board) sign-off before the NCR can close.

Corrective vs preventive. A corrective action fixes the parts in front of you (re-inspect the batch, scrap the bad ones). A preventive action changes the process so it can't happen again (add a fixture, update the procedure). The heavier workflows make you do both — and Carbon won't let you close the NCR until every required action is checked off.

Raise an NCR

Here's the click-by-click for opening a non-conformance — say a handful of Counterweight Hooks fail the dimensional check at operation 30.

Start the NCR from where the problem is. Open the failing operation on job J000097 (or go to Quality → Issues → New). Starting from the operation pre-fills the link back to the job, the part CWH-A36-15, and the work center, so the NCR is anchored to the work.

Pick the NCR type. Choose the cause — here Manufacturing Defect if it's a cutting problem, or Material Issue if the plate was bad. This is the field you'll report on later.

Choose the workflow. Select CAPA for a recurring defect, or 8D if scrap parts may have shipped and the customer is exposed. Carbon then lists the required actions you'll have to complete.

Describe and quantify. Write what you saw, attach a photo, and record the non-conforming quantity. If you're scrapping parts, pick a scrap reason so the cost lands in the right account.

Assign and investigate. Assign an owner, then work the required actions — root cause, corrective action, and (for CAPA/8D) a preventive action and verification step. Each action is a checklist item with its own owner and status.

Close it. Once every required action is complete — and the MRB has signed off, if the workflow demands it — close the NCR. It stays on the record forever, linked to the job and the part.

Gauges and calibration: trust your measurements

An inspection is only as good as the tool doing the measuring. A gauge is any measuring instrument — a micrometer, a height gauge, a thread plug. Over time gauges drift, so each one is calibrated on a schedule against a known standard. Carbon tracks every gauge, when it was last calibrated, and when it's due again — and flags any tool that's overdue so it can't quietly produce bad readings.

Acme registers ten gauges, G00001 through G00010, each on a six-month calibration interval. Here's the live register — note the dates are real, so several are already overdue as of today (2026-06-15):

Each gauge record carries a calibration interval (six months here), a last calibration date, and a Carbon-computed next due date. When the next-due date passes, Carbon moves the gauge to Out of Calibration automatically — which is your signal to pull it from service until it's re-certified.

Register the gauge. In Quality → Gauges → New, give it a number (Carbon suggests the next in sequence, e.g. G00011), a description, a gauge type, and a home location.

Set the calibration interval. Acme uses six months. Carbon uses this to compute the next-due date every time you log a calibration.

Log each calibration. When the gauge is calibrated, record the date and result. Carbon rolls the next-due date forward and flips the status back to In Calibration.

Watch the overdue list. Filter the register by Out of Calibration to see what needs attention. Five of Acme's ten gauges are overdue right now — a realistic backlog for a small shop, and exactly the list a quality manager works down each week.

An out-of-calibration gauge taints every measurement made with it. If the Thread Plug Gauge (G00003, last calibrated 2025-09-03) was used to accept parts after its 2026-03-03 due date, you can't trust those results — which may itself become a Testing Failure NCR. Keeping gauges current is what makes your inspection records defensible.

Traceability and genealogy: proving what went where

Traceability (also called genealogy) is Carbon's ability to follow a part backward and forward through its whole life: which raw material went into it, which job made it, and which customer it shipped to. It's the payoff for all the structure above. The enabler is tracking set on the item — and the hero thread shows both flavors:

  • Batch tracking groups material by lot. A36 Steel Plate (A36-STEEL-PLT-1") has requiresBatchTracking: true, so every receipt records a batch — effectively the heat of steel from the mill. When the job pulled 20,000 LB for the 500 hooks, Carbon recorded exactly which batch was consumed.
  • Serial tracking gives each finished unit its own number — used when a customer needs to trace an individual part. The Counterweight Hook is inventory-tracked rather than serialized, but a safety-critical part would carry a serial through the same machinery.

Following the thread both ways

Because every one of those records links to the next, you can read the chain in either direction:

  • Backward (recall): "We think a bad heat of steel shipped. Which orders are affected?" Start at the batch, follow it forward to job J000097, then to SO000069, and you land on Radiant Aerospace — in seconds, not days.
  • Forward (provenance): "Radiant is asking for the material certs behind their hooks." Start at the shipment on AR000025, walk back through the job to the consumed batch, and you have the exact heat of A36 steel and the Ryerson PO it came in on.

Turn on tracking before you receive material, not after. Set requiresBatchTracking (or serial tracking) on the item up front, and every receipt, job consumption and shipment records the lot automatically. Switch it on too late and the early records have nothing to trace.
